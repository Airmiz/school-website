2Calculations Used in Analytical Chemistry

2.1 Concentration Units
Avogadro's number
Particles per mole — atoms, ions, or molecules.
$$N_A=6.023\times 10^{23}\;\text{mol}^{-1}$$
Molarity
Moles of solute per liter of solution; also mmol per mL.
$$M=\frac{\text{mol solute}}{\text{L solution}}=\frac{\text{mmol solute}}{\text{mL solution}}$$
Weight percent (w/w%)
$$\text{w/w\%}=\frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solution}}\times 100\%$$
Volume percent (v/v%)
$$\text{v/v\%}=\frac{\text{volume of solute}}{\text{volume of solution}}\times 100\%$$
Weight/volume percent (w/v%)
$$\text{w/v\%}=\frac{\text{mass of solute (g)}}{\text{volume of solution (mL)}}\times 100\%$$
Parts per million (ppm)
For dilute aqueous solutions, mg solute per L solution.
$$1\;\text{ppm}=\frac{1\;\text{mg solute}}{1\;\text{L solution}}=\frac{1\;\text{g}}{10^6\;\text{g solution}}$$
Parts per billion (ppb)
$$1\;\text{ppb}=\frac{1\;\mu\text{g solute}}{1\;\text{L solution}}$$
p-function
Negative log of molar concentration.
$$\mathrm{p}X=-\log[X]$$
Specific gravity / density
Used interchangeably in this course.
$$\text{density}=\frac{\text{g of solution}}{\text{mL of solution}}$$
Dilution equation
Moles of solute are conserved on dilution.
$$M_{\text{conc}}\,V_{\text{conc}}=M_{\text{dil}}\,V_{\text{dil}}$$

3Precision and Accuracy of Chemical Analyses

3.1 Central Value & Error
Mean (arithmetic average)
$$\bar{x}=\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N}x_i}{N}$$
Median
Middle result when data are arranged in order; for an even number of results, average the two middle values.
$$\text{median}=\text{middle value (or mean of the two middle values)}$$
Absolute error
Signed difference between measured and accepted (true) value.
$$E=x_i-x_t$$
Relative error
$$E_r=\frac{x_i-x_t}{x_t}\times 100\%$$

4Random Errors in Chemical Analysis

4.1 Population vs. Sample Statistics
Population standard deviation ($N\ge 20$)
$\mu$ is the population mean.
$$\sigma=\sqrt{\frac{\sum (x_i-\mu)^2}{N}}$$
Sample standard deviation ($N<20$)
Unbiased estimator using $N-1$ degrees of freedom.
$$s=\sqrt{\frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2}{N-1}}$$
Under a Gaussian distribution: $\sim 68.3\%$ of the area lies within $\pm 1\sigma$, $\sim 95.4\%$ within $\pm 2\sigma$, and $\sim 99.7\%$ within $\pm 3\sigma$.
Variance
$$\sigma^2\quad\text{or}\quad s^2$$
Relative standard deviation (RSD)
$$\text{RSD}=s_r=\frac{s}{\bar{x}}$$
RSD in parts per thousand
$$\text{RSD}_{\text{ppt}}=\frac{s}{\bar{x}}\times 1000\;\text{ppt}$$
Coefficient of variation (CV)
$$\text{CV}=\frac{s}{\bar{x}}\times 100\%$$
Spread (range)
$$w=x_{\max}-x_{\min}$$
4.2 Propagation of Error
Std. deviation of a sum or difference
$y=a+b-c+\cdots$. Add absolute uncertainties in quadrature.
$$s_y=\sqrt{s_a^{\,2}+s_b^{\,2}+s_c^{\,2}+\cdots+s_x^{\,2}}$$
Std. deviation of a product or quotient
$y=\dfrac{a\,b}{c}$. Add relative uncertainties in quadrature.
$$\frac{s_y}{y}=\sqrt{\left(\frac{s_a}{a}\right)^{\!2}+\left(\frac{s_b}{b}\right)^{\!2}+\left(\frac{s_c}{c}\right)^{\!2}+\cdots+\left(\frac{s_x}{x}\right)^{\!2}}$$
4.3 Significant Figures Rules
Sums and differences: the result has the same number of decimal places as the term with the fewest decimal places.

Products and quotients: the result has the same number of significant figures as the term with the fewest significant figures.

Logarithms: keep as many digits to the right of the decimal as there are sig figs in the original number.

Antilogarithms: keep as many digits as there are digits to the right of the decimal in the original number.

Rounding a 5: round to the nearest even digit.

5Statistical Data Treatment and Evaluation

5.1 Confidence Intervals
Confidence interval (σ unknown)
$t$ is taken from Student's $t$-table at the desired confidence level with $N-1$ degrees of freedom.
$$\text{CI}=\bar{x}\pm\frac{t\,s}{\sqrt{N}}$$
5.2 Hypothesis Tests
Small-sample $t$-test (mean vs. accepted value)
Test $H_0:\mu=\mu_0$. Reject $H_0$ if $t_{\text{exp}}\ge t_{\text{crit}}$.
$$t_{\text{exp}}=\frac{|\bar{x}-\mu_0|}{s}\,\sqrt{N}$$
Bias
$$\text{bias}=\mu_B-\mu_0$$
Pooled standard deviation
Used when comparing two means assumed to share a common standard deviation. Degrees of freedom = $N_A+N_B-2$.
$$s_{\text{pooled}}=\sqrt{\frac{(s_A)^2(N_A-1)+(s_B)^2(N_B-1)}{N_A+N_B-2}}$$
$t$-test for difference of two means
$$t_{\text{exp}}=\frac{|\bar{x}_A-\bar{x}_B|}{s_{\text{pooled}}}\sqrt{\frac{N_A\,N_B}{N_A+N_B}}$$
$F$-test (comparison of variances)
Tests $H_0:\sigma_1^2=\sigma_2^2$. Always place the larger $s$ in the numerator.
$$F_{\text{exp}}=\frac{(s_{\text{larger}})^2}{(s_{\text{smaller}})^2}$$
$Q$-test (rejection of an outlier)
$w$ is the spread (range) of the data. Reject the questionable result if $Q_{\text{exp}}>Q_{\text{crit}}$.
$$Q_{\text{exp}}=\frac{|x_?-x_{\text{nearest}}|}{w}$$

7Aqueous Solutions and Chemical Equilibria

7.1 Equilibrium Constants
General equilibrium-constant expression
For $aA+bB\rightleftharpoons cC+dD$. Pure liquids, pure solids, and excess solvent are omitted.
$$K=\frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}$$
Solubility-product constant ($K_{sp}$)
Example: $\mathrm{Ba(IO_3)_2}\rightleftharpoons\mathrm{Ba^{2+}}+2\,\mathrm{IO_3^{-}}$.
$$K_{sp}=[\mathrm{Ba^{2+}}]\,[\mathrm{IO_3^{-}}]^2$$

8Effect of Electrolytes on Chemical Equilibria

8.1 Ionic Strength & Activity
Coulomb's law (electrostatic force)
$\varepsilon$ is the dielectric constant of the medium.
$$F=\frac{q_1q_2}{\varepsilon\,r^2}$$
Ionic strength $\mu$
Sum is taken over all ionic species; $Z$ is the charge of the species.
$$\mu=\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_i [X_i]\,Z_i^{\,2}=\tfrac{1}{2}\!\left([A]Z_A^2+[B]Z_B^2+[C]Z_C^2+\cdots\right)$$
Activity
Effective concentration; $\gamma_X$ is the activity coefficient.
$$a_X=[X]\,\gamma_X$$
Thermodynamic vs. concentration $K_{sp}$
For $X_m Y_n$. The thermodynamic $K_{sp}$ is independent of ionic strength.
$$K_{sp}=a_X^{\,m}\,a_Y^{\,n}=[X]^m\gamma_X^{\,m}\,[Y]^n\gamma_Y^{\,n}$$
$$K_{sp}'=[X]^m[Y]^n,\qquad K_{sp}=K_{sp}'\,\gamma_X^{\,m}\,\gamma_Y^{\,n}$$
Debye–Hückel equation
$\alpha_X$ is the effective ion diameter (nm). Constants 0.51 and 3.3 apply to aqueous solutions at $25^\circ\mathrm{C}$.
$$-\log\gamma_X=\frac{0.51\,Z_X^{\,2}\sqrt{\mu}}{1+3.3\,\alpha_X\sqrt{\mu}}$$
Activity coefficient properties: $\gamma\to 1$ as $\mu\to 0$; $\gamma$ decreases as ionic charge increases; $\gamma\approx 1$ for uncharged species; ions of the same charge have approximately the same $\gamma$ at any given $\mu$.

10Gravimetric Methods of Analysis

10.1 Precipitation Gravimetry
Percent of analyte $X$ in a sample
$$\%X=\frac{\text{mass of }X}{\text{sample mass}}\times 100\%$$
Gravimetric percent (with gravimetric factor)
$\dfrac{a}{b}$ is the gravimetric factor — the mole ratio that converts moles of precipitate to moles of substance sought.
$$\%X=\frac{\text{mass}_{\text{ppt}}\cdot\dfrac{a}{b}\cdot\dfrac{\text{FW}_X}{\text{FW}_{\text{ppt}}}}{\text{sample mass}}\times 100\%$$

11Titrations in Analytical Chemistry

11.1 Titration Definitions
Titration error
Difference in volume between the experimentally observed end point and the true equivalence point.
$$E_t=V_{\text{ep}}-V_{\text{eq}}$$
At the equivalence point: $\text{mol}_{\text{titrant}}\times\dfrac{\text{stoich. coeff.}_{\text{analyte}}}{\text{stoich. coeff.}_{\text{titrant}}}=\text{mol}_{\text{analyte}}$.

12Principles of Neutralization Titrations

12.1 Autoionization of Water & pH
Water autoionization
$$2\,\mathrm{H_2O}\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H_3O^+}+\mathrm{OH^-}$$
Ion-product constant of water
$$K_w=[\mathrm{H_3O^+}][\mathrm{OH^-}]=1.0\times 10^{-14}\;\text{at}\;25^\circ\mathrm{C}$$
pH and pOH definitions
$$\mathrm{pH}=-\log[\mathrm{H_3O^+}],\qquad \mathrm{pOH}=-\log[\mathrm{OH^-}]$$
$$[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]=10^{-\mathrm{pH}},\qquad [\mathrm{OH^-}]=10^{-\mathrm{pOH}}$$
pH + pOH relation
$$\mathrm{pH}+\mathrm{pOH}=14.00$$
$pK_a$ and $pK_b$
$$\mathrm{p}K_a=-\log K_a,\qquad K_a=10^{-\mathrm{p}K_a}$$
$$\mathrm{p}K_b=-\log K_b,\qquad K_b=10^{-\mathrm{p}K_b}$$
12.2 Weak Acid / Base Equilibria
Acid dissociation constant
$\mathrm{HA}+\mathrm{H_2O}\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{A^-}+\mathrm{H_3O^+}$.
$$K_a=\frac{[\mathrm{A^-}][\mathrm{H_3O^+}]}{[\mathrm{HA}]}$$
Base dissociation constant
$\mathrm{A^-}+\mathrm{H_2O}\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{HA}+\mathrm{OH^-}$.
$$K_b=\frac{[\mathrm{HA}][\mathrm{OH^-}]}{[\mathrm{A^-}]}$$
Conjugate-pair relation
$$K_a\,K_b=K_w,\qquad \mathrm{p}K_a+\mathrm{p}K_b=14.00$$
12.3 Quadratic Solutions for Weak Acids & Buffers
Quadratic for a weak acid
Exact solution; $M_{HA}$ is the molarity of the acid form.
$$[\mathrm{H^+}]=\frac{-K_a+\sqrt{K_a^{\,2}+4K_a\,M_{HA}}}{2}$$
Quadratic for a buffer
$M_{A^-}$ is the molarity of the conjugate base; $M_{HA}$ is the molarity of the acid.
$$[\mathrm{H^+}]=\frac{-(K_a+M_{A^-})+\sqrt{(K_a+M_{A^-})^2+4K_a\,M_{HA}}}{2}$$
12.4 Buffers — Henderson–Hasselbalch
Henderson–Hasselbalch equation
$$\mathrm{pH}=\mathrm{p}K_a+\log\frac{[\mathrm{A^-}]_{\text{initial}}}{[\mathrm{HA}]_{\text{initial}}}$$
When the H–H approximation fails: $K_a>10^{-3}$ (i.e., $\mathrm{p}K_a<3$), or when $x$ is not small relative to the initial acid/salt concentrations. In those cases use the buffer quadratic above.
Reactions of a buffer with added acid or base
$$\mathrm{H^+}+\mathrm{A^-}\to\mathrm{HA},\qquad \mathrm{HA}+\mathrm{OH^-}\to\mathrm{A^-}+\mathrm{H_2O}$$
Worked example — ACS-style
A buffer is prepared by dissolving 0.150 mol of acetic acid ($\mathrm{HC_2H_3O_2}$, $K_a=1.8\times 10^{-5}$) and 0.250 mol of sodium acetate in enough water to make 1.00 L of solution. Calculate the pH of the buffer (a) initially, and (b) after 20.0 mL of 1.00 M NaOH has been added.
Step 1. Identify the buffer components and compute $\mathrm{p}K_a$. The species in equilibrium are the weak acid $\mathrm{HA}=\mathrm{HC_2H_3O_2}$ and its conjugate base $\mathrm{A^-}=\mathrm{C_2H_3O_2^-}$.
$$[\mathrm{HA}]_0=0.150\;\mathrm{M},\qquad [\mathrm{A^-}]_0=0.250\;\mathrm{M}$$
$$\mathrm{p}K_a=-\log(1.8\times 10^{-5})=4.74$$
Step 2 — part (a). Check that Henderson–Hasselbalch is valid: $K_a=1.8\times 10^{-5}\ll 10^{-3}$ and both initial concentrations are $\gg K_a$, so the approximation holds. Plug in.
$$\mathrm{pH}=\mathrm{p}K_a+\log\frac{[\mathrm{A^-}]_0}{[\mathrm{HA}]_0}=4.74+\log\!\left(\frac{0.250}{0.150}\right)=4.74+0.222$$
$$\boxed{\mathrm{pH}=4.96}$$
Step 3 — part (b). Strong base added to a buffer is consumed by the weak acid; track the stoichiometry in moles (volumes cancel in the H–H ratio).
$$\mathrm{HA}+\mathrm{OH^-}\;\longrightarrow\;\mathrm{A^-}+\mathrm{H_2O}$$
$$n_{\mathrm{OH^-},\,\text{added}}=(0.0200\;\mathrm{L})(1.00\;\mathrm{M})=0.0200\;\mathrm{mol}$$
$$\begin{aligned}n_{\mathrm{HA},\,\text{new}}&=0.150-0.0200=0.130\;\mathrm{mol}\\ n_{\mathrm{A^-},\,\text{new}}&=0.250+0.0200=0.270\;\mathrm{mol}\end{aligned}$$
Step 4. Re-apply Henderson–Hasselbalch with the new amounts.
$$\mathrm{pH}=4.74+\log\!\left(\frac{0.270}{0.130}\right)=4.74+0.318$$
$$\boxed{\mathrm{pH}=5.06}$$
Takeaway. Adding 20.0 mmol of strong base shifted the pH by only $+0.10$ — that is the buffer doing its job. The same 0.0200 mol of $\mathrm{OH^-}$ dropped into 1.00 L of pure water would give $[\mathrm{OH^-}]\approx 0.020\;\mathrm{M}$ and $\mathrm{pH}\approx 12.3$, a swing of more than seven units.
12.5 Titration of a Weak Acid with a Strong Base
Four regions of the titration curve:
  1. Initial: weak-acid equilibrium, use $K_a$.
  2. Buffer region (before equivalence): Henderson–Hasselbalch with mol $\mathrm{HA}$ and mol $\mathrm{A^-}$. At half-neutralization, $\mathrm{pH}=\mathrm{p}K_a$.
  3. Equivalence point: conjugate-base equilibrium, use $K_b$ on $[\mathrm{A^-}]_{\text{init}}=\dfrac{\text{mol HA}_0}{V_{\text{total}}}$.
  4. After equivalence: excess strong base dominates; $[\mathrm{OH^-}]=\dfrac{\text{mol}\,\mathrm{OH^-}_{\text{excess}}}{V_{\text{total}}}$.

13Polyfunctional Acids

13.1 Stepwise Dissociation
First and second dissociation (e.g., $\mathrm{H_2CO_3}$)
$$K_{a1}=\frac{[\mathrm{H_3O^+}][\mathrm{HCO_3^-}]}{[\mathrm{H_2CO_3}]},\qquad K_{a2}=\frac{[\mathrm{H_3O^+}][\mathrm{CO_3^{2-}}]}{[\mathrm{HCO_3^-}]}$$
CO₂ hydration to carbonic acid
$$K_{\text{hyd}}=\frac{[\mathrm{H_2CO_3}]}{[\mathrm{CO_2}]},\qquad K_{a1}^{\text{(overall)}}=K_{\text{hyd}}\cdot K_1$$
13.2 Amphiprotic Species
Acid- and base-side equilibria for $\mathrm{HA^-}$
$$K_{a2}=\frac{[\mathrm{H_3O^+}][\mathrm{A^{2-}}]}{[\mathrm{HA^-}]},\qquad K_{b2}=\frac{K_w}{K_{a1}}=\frac{[\mathrm{H_2A}][\mathrm{OH^-}]}{[\mathrm{HA^-}]}$$
[H₃O⁺] for an amphiprotic species (full form)
$C_{\text{NaHA}}$ is the analytical concentration of the amphoteric species.
$$[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]=\sqrt{\frac{K_{a2}\,C_{\text{NaHA}}+K_w}{1+C_{\text{NaHA}}/K_{a1}}}$$
[H₃O⁺] for an amphiprotic species (simplified)
Valid when $C_{\text{NaHA}}/K_{a1}\gg 1$ and $K_{a2}\,C_{\text{NaHA}}\gg K_w$.
$$[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]=\sqrt{K_{a1}\,K_{a2}}$$
Compare $K_{b2}$ to $K_{a2}$: $K_{b2}>K_{a2}\Rightarrow$ basic solution; $K_{b2}<K_{a2}\Rightarrow$ acidic solution.
13.3 Alpha (Distribution) Functions
Total concentration $C_T$
$$C_T=[\mathrm{H_2A}]+[\mathrm{HA^-}]+[\mathrm{A^{2-}}]$$
Fraction of each species (diprotic)
$$\alpha_0=\frac{[\mathrm{H_2A}]}{C_T},\quad \alpha_1=\frac{[\mathrm{HA^-}]}{C_T},\quad \alpha_2=\frac{[\mathrm{A^{2-}}]}{C_T},\quad \alpha_0+\alpha_1+\alpha_2=1$$
Diprotic α expressions
$$\alpha_0=\frac{[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]^2}{[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]^2+K_{a1}[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]+K_{a1}K_{a2}}$$
$$\alpha_1=\frac{K_{a1}[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]}{[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]^2+K_{a1}[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]+K_{a1}K_{a2}}$$
$$\alpha_2=\frac{K_{a1}K_{a2}}{[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]^2+K_{a1}[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]+K_{a1}K_{a2}}$$
General α denominator for $\mathrm{H_n A}$
$$D=[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]^n+K_{a1}[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]^{n-1}+K_{a1}K_{a2}[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]^{n-2}+\cdots+K_{a1}K_{a2}\cdots K_{an}$$
$\alpha_i$ is given by the $(i+1)^{\text{th}}$ term of $D$ divided by $D$ itself.
13.4 pH Effect on Solubility
Connecting α functions to $K_{sp}$
For a precipitate dissociating to a basic anion (e.g., $\mathrm{Ag_2CO_3}$).
$$[\mathrm{CO_3^{2-}}]=\alpha_2\,C_T,\qquad K_{sp}=[\mathrm{Ag^+}]^2[\mathrm{CO_3^{2-}}]$$

15Complexation, EDTA, and Precipitation Titrations

15.1 EDTA Equilibria
Formation constant $K_{MY}$
For the 1 : 1 reaction $\mathrm{M^{n+}}+\mathrm{Y^{4-}}\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{MY^{(n-4)+}}$.
$$K_{MY}=\frac{[\mathrm{MY^{(n-4)+}}]}{[\mathrm{M^{n+}}][\mathrm{Y^{4-}}]}$$
Total EDTA concentration
$$C_T=[\mathrm{Y^{4-}}]+[\mathrm{HY^{3-}}]+[\mathrm{H_2Y^{2-}}]+[\mathrm{H_3Y^{-}}]+[\mathrm{H_4Y}]$$
Fraction of fully deprotonated EDTA
$$\alpha_4=\frac{[\mathrm{Y^{4-}}]}{C_T},\qquad [\mathrm{Y^{4-}}]=\alpha_4\,C_T$$
α₄ in closed form
$$\alpha_4=\frac{K_{a1}K_{a2}K_{a3}K_{a4}}{D}$$
$$D=[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]^4+K_{a1}[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]^3+K_{a1}K_{a2}[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]^2+K_{a1}K_{a2}K_{a3}[\mathrm{H_3O^+}]+K_{a1}K_{a2}K_{a3}K_{a4}$$
Conditional formation constant
Constant only at the pH where $\alpha_4$ is evaluated.
$$K'_{MY}=\alpha_4\,K_{MY}=\frac{[\mathrm{MY^{(n-4)+}}]}{[\mathrm{M^{n+}}]\,C_T}$$

16–17Electrochemistry

16.1 Cell Notation & Standard Potential
Cell notation
Anode written on the left, cathode on the right; $\|$ is the salt bridge.
$$\text{anode}\,\|\,\text{cathode}\;:\;\mathrm{Zn}\mid\mathrm{Zn^{2+}}\,(aq)\,\|\,\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}\,(aq)\mid\mathrm{Cu}$$
Standard cell potential
All half-cell values must be reduction potentials. Do not multiply $E^\circ$ when balancing electrons.
$$E^\circ_{\text{cell}}=E^\circ_{\text{cathode}}-E^\circ_{\text{anode}}=E^\circ_{\text{reduction}}+E^\circ_{\text{oxidation}}$$
For a spontaneous (galvanic) reaction at standard state: $\Delta G^\circ<0$, $E^\circ_{\text{cell}}>0$, $K>1$. The standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) is assigned $E^\circ=0\;\mathrm{V}$.
16.2 Thermodynamics of Cells
Free energy ↔ cell potential
$F=96{,}485\;\mathrm{C/mol\;e^-}$, $R=8.314\;\mathrm{J/(mol\,K)}$.
$$\Delta G^\circ=-nFE^\circ_{\text{cell}}=-RT\ln K$$
Standard potential ↔ equilibrium constant
$$\log K=\frac{n\,E^\circ_{\text{cell}}}{0.0592}\quad(\text{at } 25^\circ\mathrm{C})$$
16.3 Nernst Equation
General Nernst equation
$Q$ is the reaction quotient.
$$E=E^\circ-\frac{RT}{nF}\ln Q$$
Nernst at $25^\circ\mathrm{C}$
$$E=E^\circ-\frac{0.0592}{n}\log Q$$
Nernst applied to $aA+bB\rightleftharpoons cC+dD$
$$E=E^\circ-\frac{0.0592}{n}\log\frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}$$
Worked example — ACS-style
Consider the galvanic cell at $25^\circ\mathrm{C}$:
$\mathrm{Zn}\,\bigl|\,\mathrm{Zn^{2+}}\,(0.0100\;\mathrm{M})\,\bigl\|\,\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}\,(1.50\;\mathrm{M})\,\bigr|\,\mathrm{Cu}$
Standard reduction potentials: $E^\circ(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}/Cu})=+0.34\;\mathrm{V}$ and $E^\circ(\mathrm{Zn^{2+}/Zn})=-0.76\;\mathrm{V}$. Calculate (a) $E^\circ_{\text{cell}}$, and (b) the actual $E_{\text{cell}}$ at the given concentrations. Is the cell galvanic?
Step 1. Identify the half-reactions and the standard cell potential. By cell convention, the right-hand electrode is the cathode (reduction) and the left-hand electrode is the anode (oxidation).
$$\text{Cathode:}\quad \mathrm{Cu^{2+}}+2e^-\to\mathrm{Cu},\qquad E^\circ_{\text{cath}}=+0.34\;\mathrm{V}$$
$$\text{Anode:}\quad \mathrm{Zn}\to\mathrm{Zn^{2+}}+2e^-,\qquad E^\circ_{\text{red,anode}}=-0.76\;\mathrm{V}$$
$$E^\circ_{\text{cell}}=E^\circ_{\text{cath}}-E^\circ_{\text{anode}}=(+0.34)-(-0.76)$$
$$\boxed{E^\circ_{\text{cell}}=+1.10\;\mathrm{V}}$$
Step 2. Write the overall reaction and the reaction quotient $Q$. Pure solids are omitted from $Q$.
$$\mathrm{Zn}(s)+\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}(aq)\;\longrightarrow\;\mathrm{Zn^{2+}}(aq)+\mathrm{Cu}(s),\qquad n=2$$
$$Q=\frac{[\mathrm{Zn^{2+}}]}{[\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}]}=\frac{0.0100}{1.50}=6.67\times 10^{-3}$$
Step 3 — part (b). Apply the Nernst equation at $25^\circ\mathrm{C}$.
$$E_{\text{cell}}=E^\circ_{\text{cell}}-\frac{0.0592}{n}\log Q=1.10-\frac{0.0592}{2}\log(6.67\times 10^{-3})$$
$$E_{\text{cell}}=1.10-(0.0296)(-2.176)=1.10+0.0644$$
$$\boxed{E_{\text{cell}}=+1.16\;\mathrm{V}}$$
Takeaway. $E_{\text{cell}}>0$, so the cell is galvanic (spontaneous as written). Note the direction of the shift: $Q<1$ means more reactant than product relative to standard state, which Le Châtelier predicts will raise the driving force above $E^\circ$. Whenever the question asks "what is $E$ at non-standard conditions," the workflow is always: identify cathode/anode → compute $E^\circ$ → write the overall reaction → form $Q$ → plug into Nernst.
17.1 Faraday's Law & Stoichiometry of Electrolysis
Charge / current / time
$$Q=I\cdot t,\qquad 1\;\mathrm{A}=1\;\mathrm{C/s}$$
Faraday's constant
$$F=96{,}485\;\mathrm{C/mol\;e^-}\;\;(\approx 96{,}500)$$
Moles of electrons
$$n_{e^-}=\frac{I\cdot t}{F}$$

22Introduction to Spectrochemical Methods

22.1 Electromagnetic Radiation
Wave equation (any medium)
$$v=\nu\,\lambda$$
Speed of light in vacuum
$$c=\nu\,\lambda=3.00\times 10^{8}\;\mathrm{m/s}=3.00\times 10^{10}\;\mathrm{cm/s}$$
Wavenumber
Common in IR; units are $\mathrm{cm}^{-1}$.
$$\bar{\nu}=\frac{1}{\lambda}$$
Photon energy
$h=6.626\times 10^{-34}\;\mathrm{J\,s}$.
$$E=h\nu=\frac{hc}{\lambda}=hc\,\bar{\nu}$$
Radiant power (intensity)
$$\mathrm{Watts}=\frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{m^2\,s}}$$
22.2 Absorbance, Transmittance, and Beer's Law
Transmittance
$P_0$ is the incident radiant power; $P$ is the transmitted power.
$$T=\frac{P}{P_0},\qquad \%T=\frac{P}{P_0}\times 100\%$$
Absorbance
$$A=-\log T=\log\frac{P_0}{P}=2-\log\,\%T$$
Beer–Lambert law
$a$ has units of $\mathrm{L\,g^{-1}\,cm^{-1}}$ when $c$ is in $\mathrm{g\,L^{-1}}$.
$$A=a\,b\,c$$
Beer's law (molar form)
Molar absorptivity $\varepsilon$ has units of $\mathrm{L\,mol^{-1}\,cm^{-1}}$.
$$A=\varepsilon\,b\,c$$
Worked example — ACS-style
A colored organic dye has a molar absorptivity of $\varepsilon=4.50\times 10^{3}\;\mathrm{L\,mol^{-1}\,cm^{-1}}$ at $525\;\mathrm{nm}$. When measured in a $1.00\;\mathrm{cm}$ cuvette, the absorbance of an unknown stock sample is $0.642$. A 10.0 mL aliquot of the stock is then transferred to a 50.0 mL volumetric flask and diluted to the mark. Calculate (a) the molar concentration of the original stock, and (b) the percent transmittance of the diluted solution measured in the same cuvette.
Step 1 — part (a). Solve Beer's law for $c$.
$$c_{\text{stock}}=\frac{A}{\varepsilon\,b}=\frac{0.642}{(4.50\times 10^{3}\;\mathrm{L\,mol^{-1}\,cm^{-1}})(1.00\;\mathrm{cm})}$$
$$\boxed{c_{\text{stock}}=1.43\times 10^{-4}\;\mathrm{M}}$$
Step 2 — part (b), dilution. Apply $M_1V_1=M_2V_2$.
$$c_{\text{dil}}=c_{\text{stock}}\cdot\frac{V_1}{V_2}=(1.43\times 10^{-4}\;\mathrm{M})\cdot\frac{10.0\;\mathrm{mL}}{50.0\;\mathrm{mL}}=2.85\times 10^{-5}\;\mathrm{M}$$
Step 3. Beer's law gives the new absorbance.
$$A_{\text{dil}}=\varepsilon\,b\,c_{\text{dil}}=(4.50\times 10^{3})(1.00)(2.85\times 10^{-5})=0.128$$
Step 4. Convert absorbance back to percent transmittance.
$$\%T=10^{-A}\times 100\%=10^{-0.128}\times 100\%$$
$$\boxed{\%T=74.5\%}$$
Takeaway. Beer's law is linear in concentration, so a 5× dilution gives an absorbance 5× smaller — but transmittance is logarithmic, so a 5× drop in $A$ moves $\%T$ from $22.8\%$ at the stock concentration to $74.5\%$ at the diluted concentration. Working in $A$ keeps the math linear.
22.3 Figures of Merit
Calibration sensitivity
Slope of the calibration curve.
$$\text{calibration sensitivity}=m=\frac{dS}{dc}$$
Analytical sensitivity
$s_s$ is the standard deviation of the measurement.
$$\gamma=\frac{m}{s_s}$$
Detection limit (signal)
$$S_m=3\,s_{\text{bl}}+\bar{S}_{\text{bl}}$$
Limit of quantitation (LOQ)
$$\text{LOQ}=10\,s_{\text{bl}}$$
Dynamic range extends from the LOQ to the limit of linearity (LOL, where the calibration curve departs from linearity by ~5%).

29Introduction to Chromatographic Separations

29.1 Retention Time & Linear Velocity
Retention time relation
$t_m$ = void/dead time (un-retained); $t_s$ = time spent in the stationary phase; $t_r$ = total retention time.
$$t_r=t_s+t_m$$
Average linear velocity of the mobile phase
$L$ is the column length.
$$\upsilon=\frac{L}{t_m}$$
29.2 Partition Coefficient and Ratio
Partition coefficient
Ratio of stationary-phase to mobile-phase analyte concentration.
$$K=\frac{C_s}{C_m}$$
Partition (retention) ratio — definitions
$$k'=\frac{C_s\,V_s}{C_m\,V_m}=\frac{t_r-t_m}{t_m}$$
Practical guidance: $k'<1$ rarely gives adequate separation; $k'>10$ takes too long to elute. Aim for $1\le k'\le 10$.
29.3 Column Efficiency
Effective plate number
$W_b$ is the peak width at the base.
$$N_{\text{eff}}=16\!\left(\frac{t_r}{W_b}\right)^{\!2}$$
Plate height
$$H=\frac{L}{N_{\text{eff}}}\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad N_{\text{eff}}=\frac{L}{H}$$
29.4 Resolution
Column resolution $R_s$
$W_A$, $W_B$ are baseline peak widths; $(t_r)_A<(t_r)_B$.
$$R_s=\frac{2\,[(t_r)_B-(t_r)_A]}{W_A+W_B}$$
Common interpretation: $R_s\approx 0.75$ → poor separation; $R_s\approx 1.0$ → fair; $R_s\ge 1.5$ → baseline resolved. Improve resolution by reducing the plate height (smaller particle size) or by increasing column length.
Worked example — ACS-style
On a $25.0\;\mathrm{cm}$ HPLC column, two analytes elute at retention times of $7.50\;\mathrm{min}$ (compound A) and $8.20\;\mathrm{min}$ (compound B), with baseline peak widths of $0.40\;\mathrm{min}$ and $0.45\;\mathrm{min}$ respectively. An un-retained marker passes through the column in $1.20\;\mathrm{min}$. Calculate (a) the partition ratio $k'$ for compound B, (b) the effective plate number $N_{\text{eff}}$ for compound B, (c) the plate height $H$ for compound B, and (d) the resolution $R_s$ between the two peaks. Is the pair baseline-resolved?
Step 1 — part (a). Use the partition-ratio definition with $t_m=1.20\;\mathrm{min}$ and $(t_r)_B=8.20\;\mathrm{min}$.
$$k'_B=\frac{t_r-t_m}{t_m}=\frac{8.20-1.20}{1.20}=\frac{7.00}{1.20}$$
$$\boxed{k'_B=5.83}$$
Comfortably in the recommended range $1\le k'\le 10$.
Step 2 — part (b). Effective plate number from $t_r$ and the baseline peak width $W_b$.
$$N_{\text{eff}}=16\!\left(\frac{t_r}{W_b}\right)^{\!2}=16\!\left(\frac{8.20}{0.45}\right)^{\!2}=16\,(18.22)^2$$
$$\boxed{N_{\text{eff}}\approx 5.31\times 10^{3}\;\text{plates}}$$
Step 3 — part (c). Plate height is column length divided by plate number.
$$H=\frac{L}{N_{\text{eff}}}=\frac{25.0\;\mathrm{cm}}{5310}=4.71\times 10^{-3}\;\mathrm{cm}$$
$$\boxed{H\approx 47\;\mu\mathrm{m}}$$
Step 4 — part (d). Resolution from the two retention times and the average peak width.
$$R_s=\frac{2\,[(t_r)_B-(t_r)_A]}{W_A+W_B}=\frac{2\,(8.20-7.50)}{0.40+0.45}=\frac{1.40}{0.85}$$
$$\boxed{R_s=1.65}$$
Takeaway. $R_s=1.65\ge 1.5$, so the pair is baseline-resolved — peaks are well separated for accurate quantitation. The standard "one-knob" levers if you needed to push $R_s$ higher are: smaller-particle stationary phase (drives $H$ down), longer column (raises $N_{\text{eff}}$), or a more selective mobile phase composition (changes $k'$ ratios).
