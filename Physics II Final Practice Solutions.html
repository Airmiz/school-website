Problem 1 electric potential energy → calorimetry / latent heat
A storm cloud 200 m off the ground sits over a fish pond initially at $20^\circ\mathrm{C}$. The pond is at $V=0$ and the cloud is at $V=250\;\mathrm{MV}$. A lightning strike of $50\;\mathrm{C}$ leaves the cloud and hits the pond.

(a) What is the electric potential energy carried by the $50\;\mathrm{C}$ of charge?
(b) Assuming all of that energy is absorbed by the water, how many kg of pond water can be raised to $100^\circ\mathrm{C}$ and then boiled away?
Solution
Part (a) — electric potential energy.
$\Delta\mathrm{PE}_E=q\,\Delta V$ (formula sheet: $\Delta V=\Delta\mathrm{PE}_E/q$).
$$\Delta\mathrm{PE}_E=(50\;\mathrm{C})(250\times 10^{6}\;\mathrm{V})=1.25\times 10^{10}\;\mathrm{J}$$
$\Delta\mathrm{PE}_E=1.25\times 10^{10}\;\mathrm{J}$   (12.5 GJ)
Part (b) — kg of water boiled away.
Energy per kg of water = (heat from $20^\circ$ to $100^\circ$) + (latent heat of vaporization)
$$E_{\text{per kg}}=c_w\Delta T+L_v=(4186)(80)+(2.26\times 10^6)$$
$$E_{\text{per kg}}=334{,}880+2{,}260{,}000=2.595\times 10^{6}\;\mathrm{J/kg}$$
Divide the total available energy by the energy required per kg.
$$m=\frac{1.25\times 10^{10}}{2.595\times 10^{6}}\approx 4.82\times 10^{3}\;\mathrm{kg}$$
$m\approx 4{,}820\;\mathrm{kg}$   (~ 4.8 tonnes of water)
The 200-m height is a distractor — gravitational PE doesn't appear in this part because the question gives $V=0$ at the pond and $V=250\;\mathrm{MV}$ at the cloud directly. The voltage already contains the geometry.
Problem 2 resistor network → power dissipation → magnetic field directions
A 18-V battery drives a network with the following layout: top branch $4\;\Omega$, a $12\;\Omega$ resistor in the middle (current $I_1$ flowing down), in parallel with a right column made of $3\;\Omega$ on top (Wire 1, current $I_2$ flowing down) and $1\;\Omega$ on the bottom (Wire 2). The bottom branch is a $2\;\Omega$ resistor. Point $P$ lies just above Wire 1 (top-right of the figure).

(a) Find $I_1$ and $I_2$.
(b) The $3\;\Omega$ resistor is a heater warming a cup of $30\;\mathrm{g}$ water and $10\;\mathrm{g}$ ice initially at $0^\circ\mathrm{C}$. After 30 minutes, what is the final water temperature?
(c) Draw the direction of the magnetic field at $P$ due to Wire 1 and due to Wire 2.
Solution
Part (a) — currents.
The two parallel branches between the top-mid and bottom-mid nodes are: $12\;\Omega$ alone, and $3\;\Omega+1\;\Omega=4\;\Omega$ in series. Their parallel combination:
$$\frac{1}{R_p}=\frac{1}{12}+\frac{1}{4}=\frac{1}{12}+\frac{3}{12}=\frac{4}{12}\;\Rightarrow\;R_p=3\;\Omega$$
Total circuit resistance and total current:
$$R_{\text{eq}}=4+R_p+2=9\;\Omega,\qquad I_{\text{tot}}=\frac{V}{R_{\text{eq}}}=\frac{18}{9}=2\;\mathrm{A}$$
Voltage across the parallel section:
$$V_{p}=I_{\text{tot}}\cdot R_p=2\cdot 3=6\;\mathrm{V}$$
Branch currents (each branch sees this same $V_p$):
$$I_1=\frac{V_p}{12\;\Omega}=\frac{6}{12}=0.5\;\mathrm{A}$$
$$I_2=\frac{V_p}{3+1\;\Omega}=\frac{6}{4}=1.5\;\mathrm{A}$$
Check: $I_1+I_2=0.5+1.5=2\;\mathrm{A}$ matches $I_{\text{tot}}$ ✓
$I_1=0.5\;\mathrm{A}$,   $I_2=1.5\;\mathrm{A}$
Part (b) — final water temperature after 30 minutes.
Power dissipated by the $3\;\Omega$ heater:
$$P=I_2^{\,2}\,R=(1.5)^2(3)=6.75\;\mathrm{W}$$
Energy delivered in 30 min:
$$E=P\cdot t=(6.75)(30\cdot 60)=12{,}150\;\mathrm{J}$$
First, melt the ice at $0^\circ\mathrm{C}$ (uses up some of the energy without raising temperature):
$$Q_{\text{melt}}=m_{\text{ice}}\,L_f=(0.010)(3.33\times 10^{5})=3{,}330\;\mathrm{J}$$
Energy left for heating: $E_{\text{left}}=12{,}150-3{,}330=8{,}820\;\mathrm{J}$.
All ice has melted, so now there is $30+10=40\;\mathrm{g}=0.040\;\mathrm{kg}$ of water at $0^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Heat it with the remaining energy:
$$Q=m\,c_w\,\Delta T\;\Rightarrow\;\Delta T=\frac{8{,}820}{(0.040)(4186)}=\frac{8{,}820}{167.44}\approx 52.7^\circ\mathrm{C}$$
Sanity check: heating $0.040\;\mathrm{kg}$ from $0^\circ$ all the way to $100^\circ$ would need $0.040\cdot 4186\cdot 100=16{,}744\;\mathrm{J}$ — more than the $8{,}820\;\mathrm{J}$ available, so the water never reaches boiling. Good.
$T_{\text{final}}\approx 52.7^\circ\mathrm{C}$
Part (c) — magnetic field directions at $P$.
Wire 1 ($3\;\Omega$, vertical, current $I_2$ flowing downward). $P$ is to the upper-right. Right-hand rule: thumb down, fingers wrap. To the right of the wire, the field points out of the page.
Wire 2 ($1\;\Omega$, horizontal at the bottom, carrying $I_2$). The current enters Wire 2 from the bottom of Wire 1 and returns to the bottom-mid node, so it flows right-to-left. $P$ is far above this wire. Right-hand rule: thumb left, fingers wrap. Above the wire, the field points into the page.
$\mathbf{B}_{\text{Wire 1}}$ at $P$: out of the page (toward you)  ⊙
$\mathbf{B}_{\text{Wire 2}}$ at $P$: into the page (away from you)  ⊗
Problem 3 parallel-plate capacitor → proton acceleration → motion in $\vec{B}$
Two large conductor plates are connected to a 9-V battery; the plate separation is $d=0.15\;\mathrm{m}$. A proton released at plate A passes through a slit in plate B and enters a region of uniform $\vec{B}=2\;\mathrm{T}$ (red area). On entering, the proton curves upward. Gravity is negligible.

(a) Surface charge density on each plate (with sign).
(b) Magnitude and direction of the electric force on the proton between the plates.
(c) Magnitude and direction of the magnetic force when the proton just enters the red area (the field is perpendicular to the proton's velocity at entry).
(d) Direction of $\vec{B}$ in the red area.
Solution
Part (a) — surface charge densities.
For ideal parallel plates, $E$ between them is uniform with $E=V/d$:
$$E=\frac{V}{d}=\frac{9}{0.15}=60\;\mathrm{V/m}$$
Between two oppositely charged parallel plates, $E=\sigma/\varepsilon_0$:
$$\sigma=\varepsilon_0\,E=(8.85\times 10^{-12})(60)=5.31\times 10^{-10}\;\mathrm{C/m^{2}}$$
Sign: the proton starts at A and accelerates toward B, so $\vec{E}$ points from A → B. That requires plate A to be at higher potential (positive) and plate B at lower potential (negative).
Plate A: $\sigma_A=+5.31\times 10^{-10}\;\mathrm{C/m^2}$   (positive)
Plate B: $\sigma_B=-5.31\times 10^{-10}\;\mathrm{C/m^2}$   (negative)
Part (b) — electric force between plates.
$$F_E=qE=(1.6\times 10^{-19})(60)=9.6\times 10^{-18}\;\mathrm{N}$$
Direction: from A → B (the same direction as the proton's acceleration; rightward in the figure).
$F_E=9.6\times 10^{-18}\;\mathrm{N}$, directed from plate A toward plate B.
Part (c) — magnetic force at the entry into the red area.
Speed at plate B from energy conservation $qV=\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$:
$$v=\sqrt{\frac{2qV}{m_p}}=\sqrt{\frac{2(1.6\times 10^{-19})(9)}{1.67\times 10^{-27}}}=\sqrt{1.72\times 10^{9}}\approx 4.15\times 10^{4}\;\mathrm{m/s}$$
With $\vec{B}\perp\vec{v}$ (so $\sin\theta=1$):
$$F_B=qvB=(1.6\times 10^{-19})(4.15\times 10^{4})(2)\approx 1.33\times 10^{-14}\;\mathrm{N}$$
Direction: the trajectory curves upward at entry, so the magnetic force at that instant points upward (it's the centripetal force initially).
$F_B\approx 1.33\times 10^{-14}\;\mathrm{N}$, directed upward at the moment of entry.
Part (d) — direction of $\vec{B}$ in the red area.
Use $\vec{F}_B=q\vec{v}\times\vec{B}$. At entry $\vec{v}=v\hat{x}$ (rightward) and the force is $+\hat{y}$ (upward). For a positive charge:
$$\hat{x}\times\vec{B}=+\hat{y}\;\Longrightarrow\;\vec{B}=-\hat{z}$$
(because $\hat{x}\times(-\hat{z})=+\hat{y}$).
$\vec{B}$ points into the page.
Problem 4(a) convex (diverging) mirror — location, height, type, orientation
A 3-cm-tall object is placed 10 cm in front of a convex mirror with focal length 20 cm. Find the location and height of the image, and state whether the image is real or virtual and upright or inverted.
Solution
Step 1. Apply the mirror sign convention. A convex mirror is a diverging mirror, so $f$ is negative: $f=-20\;\mathrm{cm}$. The object is in front of the mirror, so $d_o=+10\;\mathrm{cm}$.
Step 2. Mirror equation $\dfrac{1}{d_o}+\dfrac{1}{d_i}=\dfrac{1}{f}$.
$$\frac{1}{10}+\frac{1}{d_i}=\frac{1}{-20}=-\frac{1}{20}$$
$$\frac{1}{d_i}=-\frac{1}{20}-\frac{1}{10}=-\frac{1}{20}-\frac{2}{20}=-\frac{3}{20}$$
$$d_i=-\frac{20}{3}\approx -6.67\;\mathrm{cm}$$
Negative $d_i$ ⇒ image is behind the mirror — it's virtual.
Step 3. Magnification and image height.
$$m=-\frac{d_i}{d_o}=-\frac{-20/3}{10}=\frac{2}{3}$$
$$h_i=m\,h_o=\tfrac{2}{3}(3)=2\;\mathrm{cm}$$
Positive $m$ ⇒ image is upright; $|m|<1$ ⇒ smaller than the object.
Image is at $|d_i|=\dfrac{20}{3}\approx 6.67\;\mathrm{cm}$ behind the mirror.
Height: $h_i=2\;\mathrm{cm}$ (reduced from 3 cm).
Image is virtual and upright.
Quick sanity check: convex mirrors always produce a virtual, upright, reduced image of a real object — exactly what we got. (Same as the back of a spoon or a passenger-side car mirror.)
Problem 4(b) two thin lenses in a row — final image position
Two thin lenses with focal lengths $f_1=0.2\;\mathrm{m}$ and $f_2=0.6\;\mathrm{m}$ are separated by $0.2\;\mathrm{m}$. The figure shows lens 1 as a biconcave (diverging) lens and lens 2 as a biconvex (converging) lens. An object sits 0.6 m to the left of lens 1. Find the final image position.
Sign-convention setup. The figure shows lens 1 as diverging, so $f_1=-0.2\;\mathrm{m}$. Lens 2 is converging, so $f_2=+0.6\;\mathrm{m}$. Use $\dfrac{1}{d_o}+\dfrac{1}{d_i}=\dfrac{1}{f}$ at each lens and feed the image of lens 1 in as the object of lens 2.
Solution
Step 1 — lens 1. $d_{o1}=+0.6\;\mathrm{m}$, $f_1=-0.2\;\mathrm{m}$.
$$\frac{1}{0.6}+\frac{1}{d_{i1}}=\frac{1}{-0.2}=-5$$
$$\frac{1}{d_{i1}}=-5-\frac{1}{0.6}=-5-\frac{5}{3}=-\frac{20}{3}$$
$$d_{i1}=-\frac{3}{20}=-0.15\;\mathrm{m}$$
Negative $d_{i1}$ ⇒ image 1 is on the same side as the object (in front of lens 1) — a virtual image, 0.15 m to the left of lens 1.
Step 2 — find $d_{o2}$ for lens 2. Image 1 lies $0.15\;\mathrm{m}$ to the left of lens 1, and lens 2 sits $0.2\;\mathrm{m}$ to the right of lens 1. So image 1 is $0.15+0.20=0.35\;\mathrm{m}$ to the left of lens 2 — a real object for lens 2.
$$d_{o2}=+0.35\;\mathrm{m}$$
Step 3 — lens 2.
$$\frac{1}{0.35}+\frac{1}{d_{i2}}=\frac{1}{0.6}$$
$$\frac{1}{d_{i2}}=\frac{1}{0.6}-\frac{1}{0.35}=1.667-2.857=-1.190$$
$$d_{i2}=-\frac{1}{1.190}\approx -0.84\;\mathrm{m}$$
Negative $d_{i2}$ ⇒ image 2 is in front of lens 2 (on the same side as the original object) — a virtual image.
Final image is approximately $0.84\;\mathrm{m}$ to the left of lens 2 — a virtual image. (Equivalently, 0.64 m to the left of lens 1, since lens 2 is 0.2 m to its right.)
If your instructor intended both focal lengths positive (i.e., $f_1=+0.2\;\mathrm{m}$, both lenses converging despite the figure), repeat Step 1 with $f_1=+0.2$: $\dfrac{1}{0.6}+\dfrac{1}{d_{i1}}=5\Rightarrow d_{i1}=+0.3\;\mathrm{m}$. Then image 1 forms 0.1 m past lens 2 (a virtual object for it), so $d_{o2}=-0.1\;\mathrm{m}$, and lens 2 gives $\dfrac{1}{d_{i2}}=\dfrac{1}{0.6}+\dfrac{1}{0.1}\Rightarrow d_{i2}\approx 0.086\;\mathrm{m}$ to the right of lens 2 (real image). Pick whichever sign of $f_1$ matches your class's convention for the figure.

Formula Recap (PHY 206 final reference)

Thermal & calorimetry
$Q=mc\,\Delta T$   (heating without phase change)
$Q=mL$   (latent heat for a phase change; $L_f$ for melting/freezing, $L_v$ for vaporization)
For water: $c_w=4186\;\mathrm{J/(kg\cdot ^\circ C)}$,   $L_f=3.33\times 10^{5}\;\mathrm{J/kg}$,   $L_v=2.26\times 10^{6}\;\mathrm{J/kg}$
Electric potential, field, and force
$\Delta V=\Delta\mathrm{PE}_E/q$   ⇒   $\Delta\mathrm{PE}_E=q\,\Delta V$
Parallel plates: $E=V/d$,   $E=\sigma/\varepsilon_0$ between plates
$F=qE$   (electric force on a charge in a field)
Energy conservation: $qV=\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$ for a charge falling through potential difference $V$
DC circuits
$V=IR$,   $P=IV=I^2R=V^2/R$
Series: $R_{\text{eq}}=R_1+R_2+\cdots$
Parallel: $\dfrac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}}=\dfrac{1}{R_1}+\dfrac{1}{R_2}+\cdots$
Energy delivered in time $t$:   $E=Pt$
Magnetic force & field of a wire
$\vec{F}_B=q\,\vec{v}\times\vec{B}$,   magnitude $F_B=qvB\sin\theta$
Right-hand rule for B from a long straight wire: thumb along current, fingers curl in the direction of $\vec{B}$.
$B=\mu_0 I/(2\pi a)$ at distance $a$ from a long straight wire
Mirrors and thin lenses
Mirror / lens equation:   $\dfrac{1}{d_o}+\dfrac{1}{d_i}=\dfrac{1}{f}$
Magnification:   $m=-\dfrac{d_i}{d_o}$,   $h_i=m\,h_o$
Mirror signs: $f>0$ converging (concave), $f<0$ diverging (convex). $d_o>0$ if object is in front; $d_i>0$ if image is in front (real), $d_i<0$ if behind (virtual).
Thin-lens signs: $f>0$ converging, $f<0$ diverging. $d_o>0$ if object in front (real), $d_o<0$ if behind (virtual). $d_i>0$ if image in back (real), $d_i<0$ if in front (virtual).
Two-lens systems: the image of lens 1 is the object of lens 2. Use the inter-lens distance $L$ and the sign of $d_{i1}$ to find $d_{o2}$: if image 1 is to the left of lens 2, $d_{o2}=L-d_{i1}$ (real object); if it's to the right of lens 2, $d_{o2}$ is negative (virtual object).
Useful constants
$\varepsilon_0=8.85\times 10^{-12}\;\mathrm{C^2/(N\cdot m^2)}$,   $\mu_0=4\pi\times 10^{-7}\;\mathrm{T\cdot m/A}$
$k=1/(4\pi\varepsilon_0)=8.99\times 10^{9}\;\mathrm{N\cdot m^2/C^2}$
Proton: $q_p=+1.6\times 10^{-19}\;\mathrm{C}$, $m_p=1.67\times 10^{-27}\;\mathrm{kg}$
Electron: $q_e=-1.6\times 10^{-19}\;\mathrm{C}$, $m_e=9.11\times 10^{-31}\;\mathrm{kg}$
