Topic 1 — Treatment of alcoholism (medical & psychological)

1. What interests me

I'm very interested in the treatment and cause of addiction. My major is biochemistry, and I'm looking toward jobs/research in addiction.

2. Why it matters now

Alcoholism — alcohol use disorder (AUD) — affects roughly 29 million Americans and 209 million people globally. U.S. alcohol-related deaths run around 178,000 per year, yet only about 7% of people with AUD receive any treatment in a given year. The first-year relapse rate is roughly 40–60% (needs further verification). FDA-approved medications like naltrexone and acamprosate exist but are dramatically underprescribed, while peer-support models like AA remain the cultural default despite mixed evidence on their effectiveness. The gap between what the science supports and what patients actually receive is actively debated in addiction medicine.

3. Questions to investigate

  • Does combining pharmacological treatment with cognitive-behavioral therapy produce better long-term abstinence outcomes than either approach alone?
  • Why are FDA-approved AUD medications (naltrexone, acamprosate, disulfiram) so underprescribed by primary-care physicians?
  • How do the neurological changes from chronic alcohol use shape relapse risk even after extended abstinence — why does AUD behave as a chronic relapsing condition?
  • Do harm-reduction approaches (controlled drinking) produce better outcomes for certain patient subgroups than abstinence-only approaches?

4. Who is most impacted

Most directly, the 29 million U.S. adults with AUD and their families — but the impact is unevenly distributed. Men die of alcohol-related causes at roughly 2–3× the rate of women, though the gap is narrowing as women's drinking rises. Native American and Alaska Native populations face the highest alcohol-related mortality of any U.S. demographic group. Adults aged 45–74 carry the heaviest mortality. Beyond the patients themselves, the topic affects healthcare systems (alcohol is a leading cause of preventable ER visits and liver transplants), employers, the criminal justice system, and children of parents with AUD, who face elevated risk of developing AUD themselves.

5. Viewpoints & perspectives

  • Disease model vs. behavioral/choice model — Is addiction a chronic brain disease or a learned behavior responsive to incentives and environment?
  • Abstinence-only vs. harm reduction — AA and most U.S. treatment centers require total abstinence; European models accept moderation (e.g. methadone for opioid users).
  • Medication vs. psychosocial treatment — Should pharmacology be first-line, or are behavioral interventions sufficient for most patients?
  • 12-step vs. evidence-based approaches — AA is the most accessible support system but has historically resisted clinical evaluation. Researchers argue its dominance has crowded out better-supported methods.

6. Keywords

General topic: Addiction Treatment
Specified research topic: Medical and psychological treatment of alcohol use disorder
Keywords: alcoholism; alcohol use disorder; AUD; pharmacotherapy; cognitive-behavioral therapy; relapse prevention; Alcoholics Anonymous; AA

7. Primary source

Source: Treatment for Alcohol Problems: Finding and Getting Help — NIAAA / NIH

This NIAAA publication is a federal-government primary source that lays out current evidence-based treatment options for AUD. It covers both medications and behavioral approaches for treating AUD.

Topic 2 — F1, sustainable fuels, and the future of the ICE

1. What interests me

I love watching F1 — the races, the drama. Recently there was a rule change to incorporate more electricity into the cars and use 100% sustainable fuels. I want to know whether F1's impact reaches outside racing and what that means for other car manufacturers.

2. Why it matters now

F1's 2026 regulations mandate 100% sustainable fuels and a roughly 50/50 split between internal combustion and electric power. Formula 1 leadership has explicitly framed this as a demonstration that ICE vehicles have a future — running directly against the EU's planned 2035 ban on new ICE passenger-car sales, California's 2035 zero-emission mandate, and similar policies in the UK, Norway, and parts of Asia.

3. Questions to investigate

  • Does F1's sustainable-fuel program produce fuels that are technically and economically viable for mass-market passenger cars?
  • Why have major automakers committed to F1's 2026 sustainable-fuel hybrid platform at the same time they're committing to all-EV roadmaps?
  • Has F1's historical role as a technology-transfer pipeline (disc brakes, hybrids, aerodynamics) continued into the sustainable-fuel era, or is the transfer mostly symbolic?
  • How does the lifecycle carbon footprint of F1-developed sustainable fuels compare with battery electric vehicles?

4. Who is most impacted

Most directly, everyone who drives a car or relies on cars for food, goods, transport, or enjoyment — essentially everyone. Roughly 1.4 billion passenger vehicles are on the road globally, almost all using internal combustion. If sustainable fuels prove viable, those vehicles could potentially be decarbonized without replacement; if not, they'll need to be phased out and replaced. The impact also spreads to automakers, oil and energy companies, and anyone whose modern life depends on cars and engines. Cars are just one step toward a sustainable economy.

5. Viewpoints & perspectives

  • Sustainable fuels as a legitimate parallel path — the e-fuel industry argues e-fuels can decarbonize the existing fleet faster than replacing it, since you can't replace 1.4 billion cars in a decade.
  • The motorsport-relevance perspective — some argue F1's fuel work is genuinely transferable to road use; others say the 2026 regulations are about preserving the sport's identity, not advancing road tech.
  • Sustainable fuels as industry delay — environmental groups argue synthetic fuels are too expensive, too energy-inefficient, and primarily serve to extend ICE manufacturers' and oil companies' relevance past the point where electrification is ready.
  • The thermodynamic critique — engineers argue that using renewable electricity to make synthetic fuels and then burning them at ~30% efficiency is dramatically worse than putting that same electricity directly into a battery at ~80% efficiency.
  • The grid counter — the argument that battery manufacturing's mineral demands (lithium, cobalt, nickel), regional grid carbon intensity, and infrastructure constraints make EVs less clean than the headline numbers suggest.

6. Keywords

General topic: Cars
Specified research topic: F1's sustainable-fuels program and the future of the internal combustion engine
Keywords: Formula 1; sustainable fuels; e-fuels; synthetic fuels; 2026 regulations; internal combustion engine; ICE ban; electrification

7. Primary source

Source: "2026 F1 power unit regulations explained: More electrical power, more overtaking aids and more sustainable" — Formula 1

Published directly by F1, the article explains why F1 is moving to 100% sustainable fuel and frames the decision as deliberately road-relevant.
