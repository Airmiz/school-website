11Vectors and the Geometry of Space

11.1 Three-Dimensional Coordinate Systems
Distance between two points in 3-space
Distance from $P_1(x_1,y_1,z_1)$ to $P_2(x_2,y_2,z_2)$.
$$d(P_1,P_2)=|\overrightarrow{P_1P_2}|=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2+(z_2-z_1)^2}$$
Standard equation of a sphere
Sphere of radius $r$ centered at $(x_0,y_0,z_0)$.
$$(x-x_0)^2+(y-y_0)^2+(z-z_0)^2=r^2$$
11.2 Vectors
Component form of a vector (2D)
Vector from $P(x_1,y_1)$ to $Q(x_2,y_2)$.
$$\mathbf{v}=\langle v_1,v_2\rangle=\langle x_2-x_1,\; y_2-y_1\rangle$$
Component form of a vector (3D)
Vector from $P(x_1,y_1,z_1)$ to $Q(x_2,y_2,z_2)$.
$$\mathbf{v}=\langle x_2-x_1,\; y_2-y_1,\; z_2-z_1\rangle$$
Magnitude (norm, length) of a vector
Length of $\mathbf{v}=\langle v_1,v_2,v_3\rangle$.
$$\|\mathbf{v}\|=\sqrt{v_1^{\,2}+v_2^{\,2}+v_3^{\,2}}$$
Vector addition and scalar multiplication
For $\mathbf{u}=\langle u_1,u_2,u_3\rangle$, $\mathbf{v}=\langle v_1,v_2,v_3\rangle$, scalar $k$.
$$\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}=\langle u_1+v_1,\,u_2+v_2,\,u_3+v_3\rangle,\qquad k\mathbf{u}=\langle ku_1,ku_2,ku_3\rangle$$
Standard unit vector decomposition
Any vector written in terms of $\mathbf{i},\mathbf{j},\mathbf{k}$.
$$\mathbf{v}=\langle v_1,v_2,v_3\rangle=v_1\mathbf{i}+v_2\mathbf{j}+v_3\mathbf{k}$$
Direction (unit) vector and length-direction split
Every nonzero vector equals its length times its direction.
$$\mathbf{v}=\|\mathbf{v}\|\,\frac{\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|}$$
11.3 The Dot Product
Dot (inner / scalar) product
For $\mathbf{u}=\langle u_1,u_2,u_3\rangle$, $\mathbf{v}=\langle v_1,v_2,v_3\rangle$.
$$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}=u_1v_1+u_2v_2+u_3v_3$$
Angle between two vectors
$\theta$ is the angle between nonzero $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$.
$$\cos\theta=\frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|\,\|\mathbf{v}\|},\qquad \theta=\arccos\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|\,\|\mathbf{v}\|}\right)$$
Orthogonality test
Vectors are perpendicular iff their dot product is zero.
$$\mathbf{u}\perp\mathbf{v}\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}=0$$
Algebraic properties of the dot product
For any vectors $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}$ and scalar $c$.
$$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u},\qquad (c\mathbf{u})\cdot\mathbf{v}=c(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}),\qquad \mathbf{u}\cdot(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w})=\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{w}$$
$$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{u}=\|\mathbf{u}\|^2,\qquad \vec{0}\cdot\mathbf{u}=0$$
Vector projection of $\mathbf{u}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$
The "shadow" of $\mathbf{u}$ on $\mathbf{v}$.
$$\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}(\mathbf{u})=\left(\frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|^2}\right)\mathbf{v}$$
Scalar projection (scalar component)
Signed length of the projection of $\mathbf{u}$ in the direction of $\mathbf{v}$.
$$\mathbf{u}\cdot\frac{\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|}=\frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|}$$
11.4 The Cross Product
$2\times 2$ determinant
Building block for the cross product.
$$\det\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\ c&d\end{bmatrix}=\begin{vmatrix}a&b\\ c&d\end{vmatrix}=ad-bc$$
$3\times 3$ determinant by cofactor expansion
Expand across the first row.
$$\det\!\begin{bmatrix}a&b&c\\ d&e&f\\ g&h&i\end{bmatrix}=a\begin{vmatrix}e&f\\ h&i\end{vmatrix}-b\begin{vmatrix}d&f\\ g&i\end{vmatrix}+c\begin{vmatrix}d&e\\ g&h\end{vmatrix}$$
Cross (vector) product
$\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}$ is orthogonal to both $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ (right-hand rule).
$$\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}=\begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\ u_1&u_2&u_3\\ v_1&v_2&v_3\end{vmatrix}$$
Algebraic properties of the cross product
For vectors $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}$ and scalars $r,s$.
$$(r\mathbf{u})\times(s\mathbf{v})=(rs)(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}),\qquad \mathbf{u}\times(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w})=(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v})+(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{w})$$
$$\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{u}=-(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}),\qquad \vec{0}\times\mathbf{u}=\vec{0}$$
$$\mathbf{u}\times(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w})=(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{w})\mathbf{v}-(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})\mathbf{w}$$
Parallel test
Two vectors are parallel iff their cross product is zero.
$$\mathbf{u}\parallel\mathbf{v}\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}=\vec{0}$$
Magnitude of the cross product / area of parallelogram
$\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$.
$$\|\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}\|=\|\mathbf{u}\|\,\|\mathbf{v}\|\sin\theta\;=\;\text{Area of parallelogram}$$
Triple scalar product / volume of parallelepiped
Volume of the parallelepiped spanned by $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}$.
$$|(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v})\cdot\mathbf{w}|=\left|\det\!\begin{bmatrix}u_1&u_2&u_3\\ v_1&v_2&v_3\\ w_1&w_2&w_3\end{bmatrix}\right|$$
11.5 Lines and Planes in Space
Vector equation of a line
Line through $P_0(x_0,y_0,z_0)$ parallel to $\mathbf{v}$.
$$\mathbf{r}(t)=\mathbf{r}_0+t\mathbf{v},\qquad -\infty<t<\infty$$
Parametric equations of a line
Line through $P_0$ parallel to $\mathbf{v}=\langle v_1,v_2,v_3\rangle$.
$$x(t)=x_0+tv_1,\quad y(t)=y_0+tv_2,\quad z(t)=z_0+tv_3$$
Distance from a point to a line
Distance from point $S$ to a line through $P$ parallel to $\mathbf{v}$.
$$d(S,L)=\frac{\|\overrightarrow{PS}\times\mathbf{v}\|}{\|\mathbf{v}\|}$$
Equation of a plane (point-normal form)
Plane through $P_0$ with normal vector $\mathbf{n}=\langle A,B,C\rangle$.
$$A(x-x_0)+B(y-y_0)+C(z-z_0)=0$$
Equation of a plane (general form)
$D=Ax_0+By_0+Cz_0$.
$$Ax+By+Cz=D$$
Distance from a point to a plane
Magnitude of the scalar projection of $\overrightarrow{PS}$ onto $\mathbf{n}$.
$$d(S,M)=\left|\overrightarrow{PS}\cdot\frac{\mathbf{n}}{\|\mathbf{n}\|}\right|$$
Angle between two planes
Acute angle between their normal vectors $\mathbf{n}_1,\mathbf{n}_2$.
$$\cos\theta=\frac{|\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2|}{\|\mathbf{n}_1\|\,\|\mathbf{n}_2\|}$$
11.6 Cylinders and Quadric Surfaces
2D conic-section building blocks
Circle
$$x^2+y^2=r^2$$
Ellipse
$$\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1$$
Parabola
$$y=ax^2$$
Hyperbola
$$\frac{y^2}{a^2}-\frac{x^2}{b^2}=1$$
Standard quadric surfaces
Ellipsoid
$$\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}+\frac{z^2}{c^2}=1$$
Elliptical paraboloid
$$\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}=\frac{z}{c}$$
Elliptical cone
$$\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}=\frac{z^2}{c^2}$$
Hyperboloid of one sheet
$$\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}-\frac{z^2}{c^2}=1$$
Hyperboloid of two sheets
$$\frac{z^2}{c^2}-\frac{x^2}{a^2}-\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1$$
Hyperbolic paraboloid
$$\frac{y^2}{b^2}-\frac{x^2}{a^2}=\frac{z}{c},\quad c>0$$

12Vector-Valued Functions and Motion in Space

12.1 Curves in Space and Their Tangents
Vector-valued function
Position vector tracing a curve in 3-space.
$$\mathbf{r}(t)=\langle f(t),g(t),h(t)\rangle=f(t)\mathbf{i}+g(t)\mathbf{j}+h(t)\mathbf{k}$$
Limit (component-wise)
$$\lim_{t\to t_0}\mathbf{r}(t)=\Big\langle\lim_{t\to t_0}f(t),\;\lim_{t\to t_0}g(t),\;\lim_{t\to t_0}h(t)\Big\rangle$$
Derivative of a vector function
Always points in the direction of motion.
$$\mathbf{r}'(t)=\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}=\langle f'(t),g'(t),h'(t)\rangle$$
Velocity, speed, acceleration
For a particle with position $\mathbf{r}(t)$.
$$\mathbf{v}(t)=\mathbf{r}'(t),\qquad \text{speed}=\|\mathbf{v}(t)\|=\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|,\qquad \mathbf{a}(t)=\mathbf{v}'(t)=\mathbf{r}''(t)$$
Direction of motion
$$\frac{\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|}$$
Differentiation rules for vector functions
Product, cross-product, and chain rules.
$$\frac{d}{dt}\big[\mathbf{u}(t)\cdot\mathbf{v}(t)\big]=\mathbf{u}'(t)\cdot\mathbf{v}(t)+\mathbf{u}(t)\cdot\mathbf{v}'(t)$$
$$\frac{d}{dt}\big[\mathbf{u}(t)\times\mathbf{v}(t)\big]=\mathbf{u}'(t)\times\mathbf{v}(t)+\mathbf{u}(t)\times\mathbf{v}'(t)$$
$$\frac{d}{dt}\big[\mathbf{u}(f(t))\big]=f'(t)\,\mathbf{u}'(f(t))$$
Constant-length theorem
If $\|\mathbf{r}(t)\|$ is constant, position and velocity are orthogonal.
$$\mathbf{r}(t)\cdot\mathbf{r}'(t)=0$$
12.2 Integrals of Vector Functions; Projectile Motion
Indefinite integral of a vector function
$$\int\mathbf{r}(t)\,dt=\mathbf{R}(t)+\mathbf{C}$$
Definite integral (component-wise)
$$\int_a^b\mathbf{r}(t)\,dt=\Big\langle\int_a^b f(t)\,dt,\;\int_a^b g(t)\,dt,\;\int_a^b h(t)\,dt\Big\rangle$$
Newton's Second Law
$$\mathbf{F}=m\,\mathbf{a}=m\,\frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2}=m\,\mathbf{r}''(t)$$
Ideal projectile motion (launch from origin)
Initial speed $\|\mathbf{v}_0\|$, launch angle $\alpha$, gravity $g$.
$$\mathbf{r}(t)=\Big\langle\|\mathbf{v}_0\|\cos(\alpha)\,t,\;\;\|\mathbf{v}_0\|\sin(\alpha)\,t-\tfrac{1}{2}gt^2\Big\rangle$$
Projectile path in $xy$-form
After eliminating the parameter $t$.
$$y=-\!\left(\frac{g}{2\|\mathbf{v}_0\|^2\cos^2(\alpha)}\right)\!x^2+\tan(\alpha)\,x$$
Maximum height condition
Vertical velocity equals zero.
$$\frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\|\mathbf{v}_0\|\sin(\alpha)\,t-\tfrac{1}{2}gt^2\right)=0$$
Total flight time condition
Vertical position returns to zero.
$$\|\mathbf{v}_0\|\sin(\alpha)\,t-\tfrac{1}{2}gt^2=0$$
Projectile motion with general initial position
Initial position $\mathbf{r}_0=\langle x_0,y_0\rangle$.
$$\mathbf{r}(t)=\Big\langle x_0+\|\mathbf{v}_0\|\cos(\alpha)\,t,\;\;y_0+\|\mathbf{v}_0\|\sin(\alpha)\,t-\tfrac{1}{2}gt^2\Big\rangle$$
12.3 Arclength in Space
Arclength of a smooth curve
Curve traced exactly once on $[a,b]$.
$$L=\int_a^b\sqrt{\big(x'(t)\big)^2+\big(y'(t)\big)^2+\big(z'(t)\big)^2}\;dt=\int_a^b\|\mathbf{v}(t)\|\,dt$$
Arclength function
Distance traveled from $t_0$ to $t$.
$$s(t)=\int_{t_0}^{t}\|\mathbf{v}(\tau)\|\,d\tau$$
Speed as derivative of arclength
$$\frac{ds}{dt}=\|\mathbf{v}(t)\|$$
Unit tangent vector
Directional unit vector along the curve.
$$\mathbf{T}(t)=\frac{\mathbf{v}(t)}{\|\mathbf{v}(t)\|}=\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{ds}$$
12.4 Curvature and Normal Vectors of a Curve
Curvature (definition)
Rate at which the unit tangent changes direction.
$$\kappa=\left\|\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds}\right\|$$
Curvature in terms of $t$
$$\kappa=\frac{1}{\|\mathbf{v}\|}\left\|\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{dt}\right\|=\frac{\|\mathbf{T}'(t)\|}{\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|}$$
Curvature for a 3-space curve
$$\kappa(t)=\frac{\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\times\mathbf{r}''(t)\|}{\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|^3}$$
Curvature for a plane curve $y=f(x)$
$$\kappa(x)=\frac{|f''(x)|}{\big(1+(f'(x))^2\big)^{3/2}}$$
Curvature for a parametric plane curve $\mathbf{r}(t)=\langle x(t),y(t)\rangle$
$$\kappa(t)=\frac{|x'(t)y''(t)-x''(t)y'(t)|}{\big((x'(t))^2+(y'(t))^2\big)^{3/2}}$$
Principal unit normal vector
Orthogonal to $\mathbf{T}$ and points toward the direction of turning.
$$\mathbf{N}(s)=\frac{1}{\kappa}\,\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds},\qquad \mathbf{N}(t)=\frac{\mathbf{T}'(t)}{\|\mathbf{T}'(t)\|}$$
12.5 Tangential and Normal Components of Acceleration
Binormal vector
Completes the Frenet (TNB) frame.
$$\mathbf{B}=\mathbf{T}\times\mathbf{N}$$
Acceleration in the Frenet frame
$$\mathbf{a}=a_T\mathbf{T}+a_N\mathbf{N}$$
Tangential scalar component of acceleration
Rate of change of speed.
$$a_T=\frac{d^2 s}{dt^2}=\frac{d}{dt}\|\mathbf{v}\|=\frac{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{a}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|}$$
Normal scalar component of acceleration
Rate of change of direction.
$$a_N=\kappa\!\left(\frac{ds}{dt}\right)^2=\kappa\,\|\mathbf{v}\|^2=\frac{\|\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{a}\|}{\|\mathbf{v}\|}$$
Pythagorean shortcut for $a_N$
When $\mathbf{a}$ and $a_T$ are already known.
$$\|\mathbf{a}\|^2=a_T^{\,2}+a_N^{\,2}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad a_N=\sqrt{\|\mathbf{a}\|^2-a_T^{\,2}}$$

13Partial Derivatives

13.1 Functions of Several Variables
Volume of a cylinder (sample multivariate function)
$$V(r,h)=\pi r^2 h$$
Level curve of $z=f(x,y)$
Set of points where $f$ has constant value $c$.
$$f(x,y)=c$$
Level surface of $w=f(x,y,z)$
$$f(x,y,z)=c$$
13.2 Limits and Continuity in Higher Dimensions
Limit of a bivariate function
Must yield the same value along every path in $D_f$.
$$\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)}f(x,y)=L$$
Continuity at a point
All three conditions must hold.
$$f(x_0,y_0)\text{ exists},\qquad \lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)}f(x,y)\text{ exists},\qquad \lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)}f(x,y)=f(x_0,y_0)$$
Two-Path Test for Nonexistence: If $f(x,y)$ has different limits along two different paths to $(x_0,y_0)$, then $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)}f(x,y)$ does not exist.
13.3 Partial Derivatives
Partial derivative with respect to $x$ (definition)
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,y)=f_x(x,y)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h,y)-f(x,y)}{h}$$
Second-order partial derivatives
Four possible second-order combinations.
$$f_{xx}=\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2},\qquad f_{yy}=\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2}$$
$$f_{xy}=(f_x)_y=\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y\,\partial x},\qquad f_{yx}=(f_y)_x=\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x\,\partial y}$$
Clairaut's Theorem (mixed partials)
When $f, f_x, f_y, f_{xy}, f_{yx}$ are continuous near $(a,b)$.
$$f_{xy}(a,b)=f_{yx}(a,b)$$
13.4 The Chain Rule
Chain rule: one independent, two intermediate variables
$w=f(x(t),y(t))$.
$$\frac{dw}{dt}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt}$$
Chain rule: one independent, three intermediate variables
$w=f(x(t),y(t),z(t))$.
$$\frac{dw}{dt}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\frac{dz}{dt}$$
Chain rule: two independent, three intermediate variables
$w=f(x(r,s),y(r,s),z(r,s))$.
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial r}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial r}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial r}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\frac{\partial z}{\partial r}$$
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial s}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial s}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\frac{\partial z}{\partial s}$$
13.5 Directional Derivatives and Gradient Vectors
Gradient of a bivariate function
$$\nabla f(x,y)=\langle f_x(x,y),f_y(x,y)\rangle=\Big\langle\frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\Big\rangle$$
Directional derivative
Rate of change of $f$ in the direction of unit vector $\mathbf{u}=\langle u_1,u_2\rangle$.
$$D_{\mathbf{u}}f(x,y)=\nabla f(x,y)\cdot\mathbf{u}=f_x u_1+f_y u_2$$
Directional derivative via angle
$\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf{u}$ and $\nabla f$.
$$D_{\mathbf{u}}f(x,y)=\|\nabla f\|\cos\theta$$
Maximum / minimum rates of change
Greatest increase points along $\nabla f$; greatest decrease along $-\nabla f$.
$$\max D_{\mathbf{u}}f=\|\nabla f\|\quad\text{at}\quad \mathbf{u}=\frac{\nabla f}{\|\nabla f\|}$$
$$\min D_{\mathbf{u}}f=-\|\nabla f\|\quad\text{at}\quad \mathbf{u}=-\frac{\nabla f}{\|\nabla f\|}$$
Tangent line to a level curve at $(x_0,y_0)$
$$f_x(x_0,y_0)(x-x_0)+f_y(x_0,y_0)(y-y_0)=0$$
Chain rule along a path (gradient form)
$$\frac{d}{dt}f(\mathbf{r}(t))=\nabla f(\mathbf{r}(t))\cdot\mathbf{r}'(t)$$
13.6 Tangent Planes and Differentials
Tangent plane to a level surface $f(x,y,z)=c$
Plane through $P_0(x_0,y_0,z_0)$ with normal $\nabla f(P_0)$.
$$f_x(P_0)(x-x_0)+f_y(P_0)(y-y_0)+f_z(P_0)(z-z_0)=0$$
Tangent plane to the graph $z=f(x,y)$
At the point $(x_0,y_0,f(x_0,y_0))$.
$$f_x(x_0,y_0)(x-x_0)+f_y(x_0,y_0)(y-y_0)-(z-z_0)=0$$
Linearization (standard linear approximation)
$$L(x,y)=f(x_0,y_0)+f_x(x_0,y_0)(x-x_0)+f_y(x_0,y_0)(y-y_0)$$
$$L(x,y)=f(x_0,y_0)+\nabla f(x_0,y_0)\cdot\langle x-x_0,\,y-y_0\rangle$$
Differential (single-variable)
$$df=f'(x_0)\,dx$$
Total differential of $f(x,y)$
$$df=\nabla f(x_0,y_0)\cdot\langle dx,dy\rangle=f_x(x_0,y_0)\,dx+f_y(x_0,y_0)\,dy$$
13.7 Extreme Values and Saddle Points
First Derivative Test for local extrema
Necessary condition at an interior local max/min.
$$f_x(a,b)=0\quad\text{and}\quad f_y(a,b)=0$$
Critical points
Either $\nabla f(a,b)=\langle 0,0\rangle$, or $f_x$ or $f_y$ does not exist at $(a,b)$.
$$\nabla f(a,b)=\langle 0,0\rangle$$
Discriminant / Hessian
$$\mathbf{H}f(x,y)=\begin{vmatrix}f_{xx}&f_{xy}\\ f_{yx}&f_{yy}\end{vmatrix}=f_{xx}f_{yy}-(f_{xy})^2$$
Second Derivative Test. Suppose $f_x(a,b)=f_y(a,b)=0$ and the second partials are continuous near $(a,b)$.
  • $\mathbf{H}f(a,b)>0$ and $f_{xx}(a,b)<0\;\Rightarrow$ local maximum.
  • $\mathbf{H}f(a,b)>0$ and $f_{xx}(a,b)>0\;\Rightarrow$ local minimum.
  • $\mathbf{H}f(a,b)<0\;\Rightarrow$ saddle point.
  • $\mathbf{H}f(a,b)=0\;\Rightarrow$ test inconclusive.
13.8 Lagrange Multipliers
Lagrange's condition (one constraint)
At a constrained extremum, $\nabla f$ is parallel to $\nabla g$.
$$\nabla f=\lambda\,\nabla g$$
Lagrange multiplier system (one constraint)
Solve simultaneously for $x,y,z,\lambda$.
$$\begin{cases}\nabla f=\lambda\,\nabla g\\ g(x,y,z)=0\end{cases}$$
Lagrange multiplier system (two constraints)
Solve for $x,y,z,\lambda,\mu$ with $\nabla g_1\not\parallel\nabla g_2$.
$$\begin{cases}\nabla f=\lambda\,\nabla g_1+\mu\,\nabla g_2\\ g_1(x,y,z)=0\\ g_2(x,y,z)=0\end{cases}$$

14Multiple Integrals

14.1 Double and Iterated Integrals over Rectangles
Double integral over a rectangle
$R:\,a\le x\le b,\,c\le y\le d$.
$$\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA=\int_a^b\!\int_c^d f(x,y)\,dy\,dx$$
Fubini's Theorem (rectangular form)
Order of integration may be swapped.
$$\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA=\int_a^b\!\int_c^d f(x,y)\,dy\,dx=\int_c^d\!\int_a^b f(x,y)\,dx\,dy$$
14.2 Double Integrals over General Regions
Volume under a surface over a general region $R$
$$\text{Volume}=\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA$$
Fubini's Theorem — $y$-simple ($R$ vertically simple)
$g_1(x)\le y\le g_2(x)$, $a\le x\le b$.
$$\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA=\int_a^b\!\int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx$$
Fubini's Theorem — $x$-simple ($R$ horizontally simple)
$h_1(y)\le x\le h_2(y)$, $c\le y\le d$.
$$\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA=\int_c^d\!\int_{h_1(y)}^{h_2(y)} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy$$
14.3 Area by Double Integration
Area of a region $R$
Integrate the constant function $1$ over $R$.
$$\text{Area of }R=\iint_R 1\,dA$$
Average value of $f$ over $R$
$$\overline{f}=\frac{1}{\text{Area of }R}\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA$$
14.4 Double Integrals in Polar Form
Polar / Cartesian conversions
$$x=r\cos\theta,\qquad y=r\sin\theta$$
Fubini's Theorem (polar, $r$-simple)
Note the extra factor of $r$ in the area element.
$$\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA=\int_\alpha^\beta\!\int_{g_1(\theta)}^{g_2(\theta)} f(r,\theta)\,r\,dr\,d\theta$$
Fubini's Theorem (polar, $\theta$-simple)
$$\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA=\int_a^b\!\int_{h_1(r)}^{h_2(r)} f(r,\theta)\,r\,d\theta\,dr$$
Area in polar form
$$\text{Area of }R=\iint_R r\,dr\,d\theta$$
14.5 Triple Integrals in Rectangular Coordinates
Volume of a 3D region $D$
$$\text{Volume of }D=\iiint_D 1\,dV=\iiint_D 1\,dz\,dy\,dx$$
Fubini's Theorem (triple-integral form)
For $a\le x\le b$, $g_1(x)\le y\le g_2(x)$, $f_1(x,y)\le z\le f_2(x,y)$.
$$\iiint_D F(x,y,z)\,dV=\int_a^b\!\int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)}\!\int_{f_1(x,y)}^{f_2(x,y)} F(x,y,z)\,dz\,dy\,dx$$
Average value of $F$ over $D$
$$\overline{F}=\frac{1}{\text{Volume of }D}\iiint_D F(x,y,z)\,dV$$
14.7 Triple Integrals in Cylindrical and Spherical Coordinates
Cylindrical coordinates
Cylindrical-to-rectangular conversions
$$x=r\cos\theta,\quad y=r\sin\theta,\quad z=z,\quad r^2=x^2+y^2,\quad \tan\theta=\tfrac{y}{x}$$
Fubini's Theorem (cylindrical form)
Order $dz\,dr\,d\theta$. Note the $r$ in the volume element.
$$\iiint_D F(x,y,z)\,dV=\int_\alpha^\beta\!\int_{h_1(\theta)}^{h_2(\theta)}\!\int_{g_1(r,\theta)}^{g_2(r,\theta)} F(r,\theta,z)\,r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta$$
Spherical coordinates
Spherical-to-cylindrical conversions
$$r=\rho\sin\phi,\quad z=\rho\cos\phi,\quad \theta=\theta,\quad \rho=\sqrt{r^2+z^2}$$
Spherical-to-rectangular conversions
$$x=\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\quad y=\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\quad z=\rho\cos\phi,\quad \rho=\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}$$
Fubini's Theorem (spherical form)
Order $d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta$. The Jacobian factor is $\rho^2\sin\phi$.
$$\iiint_D F(x,y,z)\,dV=\int_\alpha^\beta\!\int_{h_1(\theta)}^{h_2(\theta)}\!\int_{g_1(\phi,\theta)}^{g_2(\phi,\theta)} F(\rho,\phi,\theta)\,\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta$$

15Integrals and Vector Fields

15.1 Line Integrals
Arclength differential ("Jacobian")
$$s(t)=\int_a^t\|\mathbf{v}(\tau)\|\,d\tau,\qquad ds=\|\mathbf{v}(t)\|\,dt$$
Line integral of a scalar field over a curve
$C$ is parametrized by $\mathbf{r}(t)=\langle g(t),h(t)\rangle$, $a\le t\le b$.
$$\int_C f(x,y)\,ds=\int_a^b f(g(t),h(t))\,\|\mathbf{v}(t)\|\,dt$$
Line integral over a piecewise-smooth curve
$$\int_{C_1\cup C_2} f\,ds=\int_{C_1} f\,ds+\int_{C_2} f\,ds$$
Contour integral notation (closed curve)
$$\oint_C f\,ds$$
15.2 Vector Fields, Circulation, and Flux
Vector field in 3-space
$$\mathbf{F}(x,y,z)=\langle M(x,y,z),N(x,y,z),P(x,y,z)\rangle$$
Line integral of a vector field
"Push" of $\mathbf{F}$ along $C$ traced by $\mathbf{r}(t)$, $a\le t\le b$.
$$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{T}\,ds=\int_a^b \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t))\cdot\mathbf{r}'(t)\,dt$$
Flow and circulation
Circulation is flow around a closed curve.
$$\text{Flow}=\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{T}\,ds,\qquad \text{Circulation}=\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{T}\,ds$$
Differential form (planar vector field)
For $\mathbf{F}=\langle M(x,y),N(x,y)\rangle$ along $C$ in the $xy$-plane.
$$\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{T}\,ds=M\,dx+N\,dy$$
Flux of $\mathbf{F}$ across a closed curve $C$
$\mathbf{n}$ is the outward unit normal to $C$.
$$\text{Flux}=\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,ds$$
Flux formula (planar, counter-clockwise $C$)
$$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,ds=\oint_C M\,dy-N\,dx$$
15.3 Path Independence, Conservative Fields, and Potential Functions
Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals
For $\mathbf{F}=\nabla f$, $C$ smooth from $P$ to $Q$.
$$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=f(Q)-f(P)$$
Conservative ⇔ closed contour integral vanishes
$$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=0\;\text{ for all closed }C\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\mathbf{F}\text{ is conservative}\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\mathbf{F}=\nabla f$$
Del operator
$$\nabla=\Big\langle\frac{\partial}{\partial x},\frac{\partial}{\partial y},\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\Big\rangle$$
Curl of a vector field
For $\mathbf{F}=\langle M,N,P\rangle$.
$$\operatorname{curl}(\mathbf{F})=\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=\begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\ \partial_x&\partial_y&\partial_z\\ M&N&P\end{vmatrix}$$
Component test for conservative fields
On a simply-connected domain with continuous first partials.
$$\mathbf{F}\text{ is conservative}\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\operatorname{curl}(\mathbf{F})=\vec{0}$$
Potential function recovery
When $\mathbf{F}=\nabla f$, the partials of $f$ match the components of $\mathbf{F}$.
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}=M,\qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}=N,\qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}=P$$
15.4 Green's Theorem in the Plane
Green's Theorem — circulation (tangential) form
$C$ is piecewise-smooth, simple, closed, positively oriented; $R$ is the region inside.
$$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{T}\,ds=\oint_C M\,dx+N\,dy=\iint_R\!\left(\frac{\partial N}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial M}{\partial y}\right)dA$$
Green's Theorem — flux (normal) form
$$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,ds=\oint_C M\,dy-N\,dx=\iint_R\!\left(\frac{\partial M}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial N}{\partial y}\right)dA$$
Area of a region by Green's Theorem
Choose $\mathbf{F}=\langle -y/2,\,x/2\rangle$ so that $N_x-M_y=1$.
$$\text{Area of }R=\frac{1}{2}\oint_C x\,dy-y\,dx$$
Key observation. Green's Theorem is the planar analogue of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus: it relates information on the boundary $C$ to information about the interior region $R$.

Find the center, $c$, and radius, $r$, of the sphere given by $$x^2+y^2+z^2-6x+2z=0.$$

Problem 10 §11.6 — match equations to quadric surfaces
Match each of the following equations to the correct quadric-surface name (Elliptical Paraboloid, Elliptical Cone, Hyperboloid of Two Sheets, Hyperbolic Paraboloid).
Solution — recognize the standard form
(a) $16y^2+49z^2=x^2$ has the structure $\dfrac{y^2}{a^2}+\dfrac{z^2}{b^2}=\dfrac{x^2}{c^2}$ — two squared terms equal to a third squared term.
$$\Rightarrow\;\textbf{Elliptical Cone}\;\text{(opening along the }x\text{-axis)}$$
(b) $y^2-x^2=4z$ has one positive and one negative squared term equal to a single linear term — the saddle.
$$\Rightarrow\;\textbf{Hyperbolic Paraboloid}$$
(c) $-\dfrac{x^2}{25}-\dfrac{y^2}{16}=z$ is two negative squared terms equal to one linear term — a paraboloid opening in the $-z$ direction.
$$\Rightarrow\;\textbf{Elliptical Paraboloid (opening downward)}$$
(d) $z^2-x^2-y^2=64$ rewrites to $\dfrac{z^2}{64}-\dfrac{x^2}{64}-\dfrac{y^2}{64}=1$ — one positive squared term minus two negatives, equal to $1$. The right-hand side $+1$ tells you it is the two-sheet variant.
$$\Rightarrow\;\textbf{Hyperboloid of Two Sheets}\;\text{(along the }z\text{-axis)}$$
(a) $16y^2+49z^2=x^2$  →  Elliptical Cone
(b) $y^2-x^2=4z$  →  Hyperbolic Paraboloid
(c) $-\dfrac{x^2}{25}-\dfrac{y^2}{16}=z$  →  Elliptical Paraboloid
(d) $z^2-x^2-y^2=64$  →  Hyperboloid of Two Sheets
Quick recognition cheat sheet for Ch 11.6. Two squared terms = one squared term → cone. One linear term and two same-sign squared terms → paraboloid (sign tells you the opening direction). One linear term and opposite-sign squared terms → hyperbolic paraboloid (saddle). Sum of squared terms = 1 → ellipsoid. Mixed signs equal to 1 → hyperboloid (one positive squared term ⇒ two sheets along that axis; two positive squared terms ⇒ one sheet around the negative axis).

Problem 7 §12.4 — curvature of a 3-space curve
Calculate the curvature, $\kappa$, for the space curve $\mathbf{r}(t)=\langle 2t,\,3t^2,\,4t^3\rangle$ at the point $(4,12,32)$.
Heads-up. The planar formula $\kappa=\dfrac{|x'y''-x''y'|}{((x')^2+(y')^2)^{3/2}}$ does not apply here — it only handles 2D curves. For a space curve use $\kappa(t)=\dfrac{\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\times\mathbf{r}''(t)\|}{\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|^{3}}$.
Solution
Step 1. Find the parameter value at the given point. From $x=2t=4$ we get $t=2$ (and $3(4)=12$, $4(8)=32$ check out).
Step 2. Compute $\mathbf{r}'$ and $\mathbf{r}''$ at $t=2$.
$$\mathbf{r}'(t)=\langle 2,\,6t,\,12t^2\rangle\;\Rightarrow\;\mathbf{r}'(2)=\langle 2,\,12,\,48\rangle$$
$$\mathbf{r}''(t)=\langle 0,\,6,\,24t\rangle\;\Rightarrow\;\mathbf{r}''(2)=\langle 0,\,6,\,48\rangle$$
Step 3. Cross product.
$$\mathbf{r}'(2)\times\mathbf{r}''(2)=\begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\ 2&12&48\\ 0&6&48\end{vmatrix}=\mathbf{i}(576-288)-\mathbf{j}(96-0)+\mathbf{k}(12-0)=\langle 288,-96,12\rangle$$
Step 4. Magnitudes.
$$\|\mathbf{r}'(2)\times\mathbf{r}''(2)\|=\sqrt{288^2+96^2+12^2}=\sqrt{92304}=12\sqrt{641}$$
$$\|\mathbf{r}'(2)\|=\sqrt{4+144+2304}=\sqrt{2452}=2\sqrt{613}$$
$$\|\mathbf{r}'(2)\|^{3}=(2\sqrt{613})^3=8\cdot 613\sqrt{613}=4904\sqrt{613}$$
Step 5. Plug in.
$$\kappa=\frac{12\sqrt{641}}{4904\sqrt{613}}\approx\frac{303.81}{121420}$$
$\kappa=\dfrac{12\sqrt{641}}{4904\sqrt{613}}\approx 0.00250$
Problem 8 §12.5 — $\mathbf{a}=a_T\mathbf{T}+a_N\mathbf{N}$ without finding $\mathbf{T},\mathbf{N}$
Consider the smooth curve $\mathbf{r}(t)=\langle 6e^t\sin(t),\,6e^t\sqrt{6},\,6e^t\cos(t)\rangle$. Without finding $\mathbf{T}$ or $\mathbf{N}$, write the acceleration $\mathbf{a}$ in the form $\mathbf{a}=a_T\mathbf{T}+a_N\mathbf{N}$ at $t=0$.
Solution — use $a_T=\dfrac{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{a}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|}$ and $a_N=\sqrt{\|\mathbf{a}\|^2-a_T^{\,2}}$.
Step 1. Differentiate using the product rule on each term.
$$\mathbf{v}(t)=\langle 6e^t(\sin t+\cos t),\,6e^t\sqrt{6},\,6e^t(\cos t-\sin t)\rangle$$
$$\mathbf{a}(t)=\langle 12e^t\cos(t),\,6e^t\sqrt{6},\,-12e^t\sin(t)\rangle$$
Step 2. Evaluate at $t=0$ ($e^0=1$, $\sin 0=0$, $\cos 0=1$).
$$\mathbf{v}(0)=\langle 6,\,6\sqrt{6},\,6\rangle,\qquad \mathbf{a}(0)=\langle 12,\,6\sqrt{6},\,0\rangle$$
Step 3. Magnitudes and dot product.
$$\|\mathbf{v}(0)\|^2=36+216+36=288\;\Rightarrow\;\|\mathbf{v}(0)\|=12\sqrt{2}$$
$$\|\mathbf{a}(0)\|^2=144+216+0=360$$
$$\mathbf{v}(0)\cdot\mathbf{a}(0)=72+216+0=288$$
Step 4. Tangential component.
$$a_T=\frac{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{a}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|}=\frac{288}{12\sqrt{2}}=\frac{24}{\sqrt{2}}=12\sqrt{2}$$
Step 5. Normal component (Pythagorean shortcut).
$$a_N=\sqrt{\|\mathbf{a}\|^2-a_T^{\,2}}=\sqrt{360-288}=\sqrt{72}=6\sqrt{2}$$
$\mathbf{a}=12\sqrt{2}\,\mathbf{T}+6\sqrt{2}\,\mathbf{N}\quad\text{at }t=0$

Problem 1 §13.2 — multivariable limits
If possible, compute the following limits. If not possible, give detailed reasoning.
(i) $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\dfrac{4e^{-5y}\sin(-2x)}{5x}$
(ii) $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(6,0)}\dfrac{\sqrt{6x-y}-6}{6x-y-36}$
(iii) $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\dfrac{y^4-2x^2}{y^4+x^2}$
Solution
(i). Split into pieces whose limits you know, using $\sin(\theta)/\theta\to 1$.
$$\frac{4e^{-5y}\sin(-2x)}{5x}=\frac{4e^{-5y}}{5}\cdot\frac{\sin(-2x)}{x}=\frac{4e^{-5y}}{5}\cdot(-2)\cdot\frac{\sin(-2x)}{-2x}$$
$$\to\frac{4(1)}{5}\cdot(-2)\cdot 1=-\frac{8}{5}$$
(i) $\displaystyle\lim=-\dfrac{8}{5}$
(ii). Direct substitution gives $0/0$. Multiply by the conjugate of the numerator.
$$\frac{\sqrt{6x-y}-6}{6x-y-36}\cdot\frac{\sqrt{6x-y}+6}{\sqrt{6x-y}+6}=\frac{(6x-y)-36}{(6x-y-36)(\sqrt{6x-y}+6)}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{6x-y}+6}$$
$$\to\frac{1}{\sqrt{36}+6}=\frac{1}{12}$$
(ii) $\displaystyle\lim=\dfrac{1}{12}$
(iii). Try two paths.
$$\text{Along }x=0:\quad\frac{y^4-0}{y^4+0}=1\;\to\;1$$
$$\text{Along }y=0:\quad\frac{0-2x^2}{0+x^2}=-2\;\to\;-2$$
Two different limits along two different paths, so the limit does not exist (Two-Path Test).
(iii) Limit does not exist.
Problem 2 §13.3 — implicit partial derivative
Determine $\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial x}$ if the equation $7xy+z^4 x-3yz=5$ defines $z$ as a function of $x$ and $y$.
Solution — differentiate both sides w.r.t. $x$, treat $y$ as constant and $z=z(x,y)$.
Step 1. Apply the chain/product rules term by term.
$$\frac{\partial}{\partial x}[7xy]+\frac{\partial}{\partial x}[z^4 x]-\frac{\partial}{\partial x}[3yz]=0$$
$$7y+\Big(4z^3\,\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\,x+z^4\Big)-3y\,\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}=0$$
Step 2. Group $\partial z/\partial x$ on one side.
$$(4xz^3-3y)\,\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}=-(7y+z^4)$$
$\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial x}=-\dfrac{7y+z^4}{4xz^3-3y}=\dfrac{7y+z^4}{3y-4xz^3}$
Problem 3 §13.4 — chain rule, answer in terms of $u$ and $v$
Determine $\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial u}$ for $z=-6e^{x}\ln(y)$, where $x=\ln(u\cos v)$ and $y=u\sin v$.
Solution
Step 1. Chain rule.
$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial u}=\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial u}+\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial u}$$
Step 2. The four pieces.
$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}=-6e^x\ln y,\qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}=-\frac{6e^x}{y}$$
$$\frac{\partial x}{\partial u}=\frac{1}{u\cos v}\cdot\cos v=\frac{1}{u},\qquad \frac{\partial y}{\partial u}=\sin v$$
Step 3. Assemble, then substitute back so that the answer is in $u$ and $v$ only. Note $e^x=u\cos v$ and $y=u\sin v$, so $e^x/u=\cos v$ and $e^x/y=\cos v/\sin v$.
$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial u}=-6e^x\ln(y)\cdot\frac{1}{u}-\frac{6e^x}{y}\sin v$$
$$=-6\cos v\,\ln(u\sin v)-6\cos v$$
$\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial u}=-6\cos v\,\big[\ln(u\sin v)+1\big]$
Problem 4 §13.5 — direction of fastest decrease
Consider the function $f(x,y,z)=\ln(xy)+3\ln(yz)+3\ln(xz)$ and the point $P(1,1,1)$.
  • (i) Find a unit vector $\mathbf{u}$ that points in the direction in which $f$ decreases most rapidly at $P$.
  • (ii) Compute $D_{\mathbf{u}}f(1,1,1)$.
Solution — simplify $f$ first using log rules.
Step 1. Expand the logs.
$$f=\ln x+\ln y+3\ln y+3\ln z+3\ln x+3\ln z=4\ln x+4\ln y+6\ln z$$
Step 2. Gradient.
$$\nabla f=\Big\langle\tfrac{4}{x},\tfrac{4}{y},\tfrac{6}{z}\Big\rangle\;\Rightarrow\;\nabla f(1,1,1)=\langle 4,4,6\rangle$$
$$\|\nabla f(1,1,1)\|=\sqrt{16+16+36}=\sqrt{68}=2\sqrt{17}$$
(i) Step 3. Direction of fastest decrease is $-\nabla f/\|\nabla f\|$.
(i) $\mathbf{u}=\dfrac{-\langle 4,4,6\rangle}{2\sqrt{17}}=\Big\langle -\tfrac{2}{\sqrt{17}},\,-\tfrac{2}{\sqrt{17}},\,-\tfrac{3}{\sqrt{17}}\Big\rangle$
(ii) Step 4. Since $\mathbf{u}$ is the direction of fastest decrease, $D_{\mathbf{u}}f=-\|\nabla f\|$.
$$D_{\mathbf{u}}f(1,1,1)=\nabla f\cdot\mathbf{u}=\frac{(4)(-2)+(4)(-2)+(6)(-3)}{\sqrt{17}}=\frac{-34}{\sqrt{17}}$$
(ii) $D_{\mathbf{u}}f(1,1,1)=-\dfrac{34}{\sqrt{17}}=-2\sqrt{17}$
Problem 5 §13.6 — tangent plane & normal line to a level surface
Consider the point $P(0,5,3)$ on the surface $S$ given by $\cos(\pi x)-x^2 y+e^{xz}+2yz$.
  • (i) Find an equation for the tangent plane to $S$ at $P$.
  • (ii) Find an equation for the normal line to $S$ at $P$.
Setup. Treat the surface as the level set $F(x,y,z)=\cos(\pi x)-x^2 y+e^{xz}+2yz=c$. The gradient $\nabla F(P)$ is normal to $S$ at $P$.
Solution
Step 1. Compute the partials.
$$F_x=-\pi\sin(\pi x)-2xy+ze^{xz},\quad F_y=-x^2+2z,\quad F_z=xe^{xz}+2y$$
Step 2. Evaluate at $P(0,5,3)$.
$$F_x(P)=-\pi(0)-0+3(1)=3,\quad F_y(P)=0+6=6,\quad F_z(P)=0+10=10$$
$$\nabla F(P)=\langle 3,6,10\rangle$$
(i) Step 3. Tangent-plane equation $F_x(x-0)+F_y(y-5)+F_z(z-3)=0$.
$$3(x-0)+6(y-5)+10(z-3)=0$$
$$3x+6y+10z=60$$
(i) Tangent plane: $3x+6y+10z=60$
(ii) Step 4. Normal line passes through $P$ along $\nabla F(P)=\langle 3,6,10\rangle$.
(ii) $x(t)=3t,\quad y(t)=5+6t,\quad z(t)=3+10t,\quad -\infty<t<\infty$
Problem 6 §13.6 — linearization $L(x,y,z)$
Find the linearization, $L(x,y,z)$, of $f(x,y,z)=\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}$ at $P(2,6,9)$.
Solution
Step 1. Evaluate $f$ at $P$.
$$f(2,6,9)=\sqrt{4+36+81}=\sqrt{121}=11$$
Step 2. Partials and their values at $P$.
$$f_x=\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}},\quad f_y=\frac{y}{\cdots},\quad f_z=\frac{z}{\cdots}$$
$$f_x(P)=\tfrac{2}{11},\quad f_y(P)=\tfrac{6}{11},\quad f_z(P)=\tfrac{9}{11}$$
Step 3. Linearization formula and simplification.
$$L(x,y,z)=11+\tfrac{2}{11}(x-2)+\tfrac{6}{11}(y-6)+\tfrac{9}{11}(z-9)$$
Constants combine to $11-\tfrac{4}{11}-\tfrac{36}{11}-\tfrac{81}{11}=\tfrac{121-4-36-81}{11}=0$ — clean!
$L(x,y,z)=\dfrac{2x+6y+9z}{11}$
Problem 7 §13.7 — local extrema & saddle points
Find all the local maxima, local minima, and saddle points of $f(x,y)=4xy+x^4+y^4$. Hint: $1-a^8=-(a-1)(a+1)(a^2+1)(a^4+1)$.
Solution
Step 1. Find critical points by setting $\nabla f=\mathbf{0}$.
$$f_x=4y+4x^3=0\;\Rightarrow\;y=-x^3$$
$$f_y=4x+4y^3=0\;\Rightarrow\;x=-y^3$$
Substitute the first into the second: $x=-(-x^3)^3=x^9$, so $x(x^8-1)=0$.
Step 2. Factor $x^8-1=(x-1)(x+1)(x^2+1)(x^4+1)$ (the hint, sign flipped). The last two factors are always positive, so real roots are $x=0,\,\pm 1$. Pairing each with $y=-x^3$:
$$\text{Critical points: }(0,0),\;(1,-1),\;(-1,1)$$
Step 3. Hessian.
$$f_{xx}=12x^2,\quad f_{yy}=12y^2,\quad f_{xy}=4$$
$$H=f_{xx}f_{yy}-(f_{xy})^2=144x^2y^2-16$$
Step 4. Classify.
$$\text{At }(0,0):\;H=-16<0\;\Rightarrow\;\text{saddle},\;f(0,0)=0$$
$$\text{At }(1,-1):\;H=128>0,\;f_{xx}=12>0\;\Rightarrow\;\text{local min},\;f=-2$$
$$\text{At }(-1,1):\;H=128>0,\;f_{xx}=12>0\;\Rightarrow\;\text{local min},\;f=-2$$
Local minima at $(1,-1)$ and $(-1,1)$, both with $f=-2$. Saddle point at $(0,0)$ with $f=0$. No local maxima.
Problem 8 §13.8 — Lagrange multipliers, two constraints
The function $f(x,y,z)=x^2+2y-z^2$ has a single global extremum — a global maximum — when subject to the constraints $6x-y=0$ and $y+z=0$. Use Lagrange multipliers to find it.
Solution
Step 1. Set up $\nabla f=\lambda\nabla g_1+\mu\nabla g_2$.
$$\nabla f=\langle 2x,2,-2z\rangle,\quad\nabla g_1=\langle 6,-1,0\rangle,\quad\nabla g_2=\langle 0,1,1\rangle$$
$$\begin{cases}2x=6\lambda\\ 2=-\lambda+\mu\\ -2z=\mu\\ 6x-y=0\\ y+z=0\end{cases}$$
Step 2. Reduce. From the constraints, $y=6x$ and $z=-y=-6x$. From the gradient equations, $\lambda=x/3$ and $\mu=-2z=12x$. Plug into $2=-\lambda+\mu$:
$$2=-\tfrac{x}{3}+12x=\tfrac{-x+36x}{3}=\tfrac{35x}{3}\;\Rightarrow\;x=\tfrac{6}{35}$$
Step 3. Back-solve.
$$x=\tfrac{6}{35},\quad y=6x=\tfrac{36}{35},\quad z=-6x=-\tfrac{36}{35}$$
Sanity check: $\lambda=\tfrac{2}{35},\;\mu=\tfrac{72}{35}$, and $-\lambda+\mu=\tfrac{70}{35}=2$ ✓.
Step 4. Function value.
$$f=\Big(\tfrac{6}{35}\Big)^2+2\Big(\tfrac{36}{35}\Big)-\Big(-\tfrac{36}{35}\Big)^2=\tfrac{36+2520-1296}{1225}=\tfrac{1260}{1225}=\tfrac{36}{35}$$
Global maximum at $\Big(\tfrac{6}{35},\,\tfrac{36}{35},\,-\tfrac{36}{35}\Big)$ with $f=\dfrac{36}{35}\approx 1.029$.
